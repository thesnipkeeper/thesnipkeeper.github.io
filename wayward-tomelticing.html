To Melt Icing

A flu had been going around the school, a nasty one that left students bedridden and miserable and in no state to do anything but cough and complain about being cold. Kade was among it all. He insisted that he could handle flu season this year, and he could. There wasn't too much to it, really, but make sure everyone was comfortable and medicated if they wanted to be.

Everyone had their own ways of dealing with illness that reflected their worlds.

Nancy showed symptoms, said nothing until she quite literally dropped to the ground with a fever of 103. Kade couldn't help but wonder if that was a sort of thing that happened often in her world. People were supposed to stand still despite any discomfort. Surely that included illness as well.

He'd helped her to her room, half-carrying her, and instructed her to get some rest.

"Thank you," she said sleepily.

"No problem," he smiled.

She was asleep before he even left her room.

Sumi, surprisingly or unsurprisingly, was difficult to deal with until she finally decided that she’d rather be in bed. She did ask for paint and paper, though, which he didn’t question.

Christopher was grumpy and quiet. He’d been one of the first to come down sick, and it was taking a toll on him.

"Morning," came the voice of Christopher, who trudged into the dining room, still in his pajamas, looking as if he hadn't slept at all.

"Morning," Kade greeted as he laid plates on the tables. "Feeling any better?"

Christopher groaned, which, Kade supposed, was just enough of an answer.

"Fever?"

He tilted his head slightly toward Kade. They'd been through this plenty of times already the past few days, as Christopher's body hadn't taken as well to fevers as others' had. So, Kade easily laid his palm across his friend's forehead. He was warm, but not alarmingly so.

"Think maybe you're on the mend," said Kade, with a smile too bright for Christopher's demeanor. "Hungry?"

Christopher huffed as he screwed his face in thought, as if that was the toughest question he'd ever been asked. "Maybe," was as good of an answer as Kade needed to get his friend a plate of food.

The two of them ate breakfast together. The dining room crowd was rather thin today, but Kade would be delivering some food to the others’ rooms in a while. Most of them were still asleep last he checked.

Christopher didn’t finish his food. Instead, he pushed it aside and used his arms as a pillow on the table.

Kade chuckled and patted his arm.

“I’m so tired,” Christopher complained, turning his head so his voice was less muffled. “And I’m so tired of being tired.”

“You’ll feel better in a day or two,” Kade promised.

“I just can’t believe that you’re not sick yet.”

-----

Kade was no idiot. When he woke to a headache and unshakable fatigue, he knew exactly what he was in for.

That was how it started with everyone else. The biggest sign that one of his friends was getting sick was when they started to become grumpy and quiet for no reason. When they fell asleep in the middle of the common room among a cacophony of noise because they were just that exhausted.

He sat up, the room immediately spinning. Oh... his limbs were jelly. The exposed parts of his shoulders and neck where the blanket had slid down caused him to shudder uncontrollably.

The mere brush of blankets against his sensitive skin was uncomfortable, he couldn't imagine what it was going to feel like to be up and about.

But... it wasn’t like he had a choice. Things needed to get done.

He groaned as he sat up, every muscle in his body complaining. Cold air that didn’t exist had him shivering like he was in the tundra.

He got dressed, slowly. He chose a more comfortable outfit that wouldn’t feel as terrible on his sensitive skin, but one that was also warm.

Kade was delighted to see Christopher dressed and heading to class, along with a couple other students who looked tired but otherwise healthy.

Now, Kade considered himself to be a generally steady, balanced person. But no one was prepared for Sumi’s hugs, no one expected to be glomped from behind in the middle of the hallway.

“Jesus, Sumi!” he exclaimed. “Warn a guy first!” A wave of dizziness and aches hit him all at once.

Sumi giggled. “You should expect it by now, silly!” and then she frowned. “Oh no, did I really hurt you?”

“N-no, I just... ‘m still getting myself together this morning.” He ran a hand down his face and sniffed. He felt a thickness in his sinuses that wasn’t there before, and certainly wasn’t helping with his headache. “Shouldn’t you be in bed?”

“I’m tired of being in bed. I’m getting breakfast.”

“I can get you breakfast, Sumi. I was heading down there myself.”

She narrowed her eyes at him and huffed. “Okay, but I trust you to put enough syrup on whatever it is.”

“What if it’s eggs or something?”

“Yeah?”

Kade shook his head. “I swear, your body must be eighty-percent sugar by now.”

A sudden itch made itself known in his throat. He tried to clear it away, but it was no use. Kade buried his face in the bend of his arm and coughed. The cough tore against his now-tender throat, making him wince in pain before he could help it.

“Oh no, you’re sick!” Sumi said, far too loudly for his comfort. He didn’t need the whole school worried about him.

“I’m-” he started defensively, and then sighed. “I’m fine right now.”

“You can always spend the day in my room,” Sumi offered sweetly.

“I don’t think Nancy would want me-”

“Nancy’s staying in the basement. I think being down in the creepy-crawly place it makes her feel better. But also! I think she loves you, so it wouldn’t matter anyway.”

Kade’s illness-gummed brain only comprehended bits and pieces of what she’d just said. “Thanks,” he said. “I think I’m okay for now. Go get some rest, alright?”

He delivered her breakfast and chose to eat his own in his room, alone. Usually, they were discouraged from taking their food to their rooms, but this week had been an exception to most of those rules.

He muffled a cough into his shoulder, and then groaned when he realized that he couldn’t eat another bite. He hadn’t even gotten through half the plate, but he felt full and gross, and his throat stung whenever he swallowed.

As nonsense as she was, hanging out with Sumi for the day was sounding better and better.

He took their plates to the kitchen and offered to help with dishes. The kitchen staff adored him and promised him an extra dessert for dinner for helping out. That extra dessert, he knew, would go straight to Sumi.

Just a few minutes over the steaming sink had him feeling woozy. He blinked a couple of times and took some deep breaths. Or, he tried to. He coughed, quickly pulling the collar of his shirt over his mouth and backing away. Stumbling away, really.

“Oh, sweetheart,” said the woman next to him. She gave him a look of pure sympathy, which he didn’t want at all. “Why don’t you go get some rest, hm?”

“Right,” he cleared his throat. He shuddered now, as his arms were still damp from the dishwater. “Sorry, I-”

“It’s alright. Go on.”

He had no intention of getting some rest quite yet. There was a lot of sewing to do today, and he- God, he felt horrible. He couldn’t believe how winded he’d gotten from the stairs. Had they always been that hard to climb?

By the time he reached the top, his airways were extremely unhappy and he was coughing again. His vision got dark at the edges for a moment, so he grabbed the closest door frame.

“Kaaaade...”

That door frame just happened to belong to Sumi.

Kade sighed and sniffled, rubbing his eyes to rid them of the watery goop that had built during his fit. He didn’t even bother to move away when he felt his friend’s hand on his forehead.

“Your skin’s hot enough to melt icing,” she said, as if that was something that actually made sense.

“I’m sure,” he said breathlessly. He pawed at his nose for a moment, and tried to blink the world back into focus. “But I’ve got things-”

“I can almost promise you that nothing will be ruined if you take a nap.”

He really, really didn’t feel good.

And the stairs to the attic looked absolutely daunting.

He sighed and gave a simple nod before going into Sumi’s room. This wasn’t the first time they’d shared a bed, nor would it be the last.

“Thanks, Sumi,” he mumbled as they got situated under the blankets. Sumi snuggled against him, cheek against his arm with a happy hum.

“That’s okay,” she said. “You’re warm.”
