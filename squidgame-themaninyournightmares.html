The Man in Your Nightmares

Debts were forgiven.

They didn’t ask questions.

It should have been a relief to be back home. To not worry about being arrested or murdered for their terrible decisions.

And it was a relief at first.

But then, they were just tired.

Being home didn’t make the guilt go away. It didn’t bring Gi-hun’s mother back, and it certainly didn’t take away the pain and numbness.

He and Sang-woo didn’t see each other again after the memorial service. They had things to figure out on their own, neither of them ready to talk about all of it. Neither of them were ready to acknowledge the pain that they went through, or the pain that they had caused.

He still went to bed at night with the image seared into his memory of Sang-woo murdering the glass maker. He still dreamt that Sae-byeok was alive (she was a thief even in his dreams. It left him feeling fond, and then incredibly pained).

Two weeks had gone by without speaking to Sang-woo, and the more time that passed, the less Gi-hun ever wanted to see the man again. All of those feelings he didn’t have time to process during the games boiled and rose to the surface.

Sang-woo, his childhood friend, was a ruthless murderer.

Plenty of people in the games killed, but Sang-woo never did it out of necessity. He did it because he wanted to win, and he was willing to do whatever he needed to do so.

Fuck him.

Gi-hun filled out an application for the local grocery, and another as a dishwasher at a restaurant. With any luck, he could work both jobs and save enough to have money in the bank for the first time in... well, a long time.

It may have been longer than two weeks - perhaps forever - that they didn’t speak, had they not run into each other at the convenience store.

Gi-hun had just wanted some dinner, a simple package of noodles and a bottle of soda.

He didn’t expect to be face-to-face with the man who’d been in his nightmares ever since the games ended.

Sang-woo nodded stiffly. “Gi-hun.”

“Sang-woo. Good to see you.” It wasn’t.

Sang-woo didn’t say anything else. He simply gestured for Gi-hun to go ahead of him in line, and Gi-hun did so.

He looked worse than Gi-hun did. He looked tired, uncoordinated. Gi-hun glanced back long enough to see him leaning on a shelf for support, as if he couldn’t hold himself up anymore. Was he drunk? Could be, and Gi-hun wouldn’t blame him.

He paid for his things and then lingered at the door. Sang-woo took the few steps up to the register with great difficulty. Confused. He looked confused. He bought some kind of medicine and handed the cashier a card to swipe. It shouldn’t have surprised Gi-hun that he was able to get back on his feet so quickly. Metaphorically, anyway.

He could leave. Just go back home, have his dinner, and pretend that he didn’t still care about Sang-woo. In the end, however, he’d never have the luxury of not caring about that fucking man. Maybe that was part of who Sang-woo was, able to force people to trust him.

But this wasn’t trust. There wasn’t a single bit of trust. Just... a sense of unity that neither of them would be able to shake after what they went through.

So Gi-hun found himself waiting outside the store.

“Sang-woo,” he said as the man exited.

Sang-woo startled and stumbled, latching onto the wall for support. He shook his head as if to clear it, and it didn’t seem to help.

“What is it?” he asked.

“Are you okay?”

“I’m fine, Gi-hun,” he breathed out tiredly.

“Neither of us are fine,” Gi-hun reasoned. “But you look...”

“I’m sick.” Sang-woo cleared his throat, and slowly stood up straight again, regaining his balance. “Nothing terrible, just a fever.”

Gi-hun frowned. His face did have a red hue to it, and his eyes were glassy and unfocused. He sighed sadly at the man. “You can hardly stand.”

Sang-woo didn’t seem to have it in him to deny it. He swallowed, which seemed to trigger a cough that had Gi-hun wincing in sympathy. That wasn’t just a fever.

He shook his head and took a couple of slow steps forward with the intent of leaving.

“Wait, let me walk you home.”

Sang-woo stopped, his shoulders falling in a sigh. “You don’t need to.”

“Let me,” said Gi-hun. “Please.”

He cleared his throat and muffled a weak cough into his shoulder. “Do what you want.”

That was as good of a confirmation as he was going to get, so Gi-hun went to his side. Now that he was close, he could hear a wheeze and gurgle to Sang-woo’s breath, and he breathed heavily as he tried to walk at a normal pace. Gi-hun didn’t say anything until the man stopped and wavered, closing his eyes in an attempt to make the world stop spinning.

He wrapped a hand around his arm and received no resistance in return. Sang-woo clearly needed help staying upright. And as they continued, Gi-hun became more and more aware of the heat seeping through Sang-woo’s jacket. He wasn’t kidding when he mentioned the fever.

Most of the walk was silent, save for when Sang-woo weakly pointed out one direction or another. Gi-hun had never been to his apartment, so he let the sick man lead him the best he was able.

He had to stop once to cough, and for a moment Gi-hun wondered if he was going to be able to stop. He patted Sang-woo’s back and offered some encouraging words that probably fell flat on the man. He reached a point where he was breathless and hunched over in the fit, and Gi-hun set hands on both his shoulders to keep him from falling on the concrete.

He tried and failed to catch his breath a couple of times before he could finally inhale without coughing. His eyes were watery, and he clumsily scrubbed at them with a sniff.

“Alright?” Gi-hun asked, feeling useless.

Sang-woo cleared his throat and nodded. “My flat is just up here,” he gestured to the next block down. He sniffled a few more times, pawing at his nose. He needed a tissue, but Gi-hun didn’t have any substitute to offer. Part of him didn’t want to go that far into caring for the man, anyway.

Sang-woo’s building was one of the nicer ones on that side of town. The elevator ride was long as they went to one of the highest floors, and Sang-woo led him down the hallway with a cough and keyed them into the apartment.

“Wow,” was Gi-hun’s reaction. “This is nice, huh?”

“I’m not sure how much longer I can afford it,” Sang-woo admitted. “But I had a couple of months paid in advance.”

Gi-hun frowned. It was strange. Even before the games, Sang-woo had looked so rich and well off. No one would have suspected that he was in complete financial ruin, that he’d put not only himself and his job in jeopardy, but his family as well. Gi-hun can’t imagine pretending like that. Anyone who took a look at him knew immediately that he was a piece of shit, so to imagine that someone could be even worse and appear so... good. Strange.

The bedroom was small, but big enough to fit a rather nice bed in it and nothing else.

“Thank you,” Sang-woo said, using Gi-hun’s shoulder as quick support as he made his way into the bedroom. He sat on the edge of the bed, dropped the bag of various medicines next to him, and clumsily tugged at his jacket sleeve. He gave up, and tried to make it look like he hadn’t just failed to undress.

Gi-hun nodded. He stood there a few seconds too long, long enough for Sang-woo to look at him with a raised eyebrow. The room was quiet enough that Gi-hun could hear the wheezing that seemed to have gotten worse since they finished their walk.

“I don’t think I can leave you alone like this,” he said quietly.

“I’ll be alright,” Sang-woo breathed out. He made a second attempt to remove his jacket, and succeeded without much coordination.

“I really want to believe that,” and he really wanted to go home and eat his shitty dinner. “But you sound... bad.”

“It’s... it’s nothing, Gi-hun. And you don’t have to worry about me.”

Gi-hun knew that, and he couldn’t help but be frustrated with himself at being reminded of it by the object of his worry. He didn’t want to care about this asshole.

But he did.

“It’s just that I’ve heard stories,” he started, shoving his hands in his pockets. Sang-woo was simply sitting there on his bed, breathing - or trying to - eyes unfocused. “About people dying of pneumonia. You know, one minute it just seems like a cold and the next they’re- they’re drowning on their own lungs. Someone finds them the next day and they’re gone!” He chuckled for some reason, when that was the last thing that the situation asked for.

Sang-woo looked at him. His glassy eyes fixed on him. His mouth formed a deep frown, brows turning up. Sympathy, a change in the dead look that Sang-woo had worn since after the first game.

Gi-hun was apparently still capable of surprising himself.

Of course he cared about Sang-woo. Because if one more person close to him died, he just might lose it.

“I don’t have any food, and... and my sofa is hard. This place really isn’t made for company.”

“That’s okay, brought my own food,” he laughed and held up the sad package that was his dinner. “And you should see my bed at home.”

Sang-woo gave a raspy chuckle at that.

“You probably need pajamas, right?”

Gi-hun didn’t give him a chance to answer before he started opening chest drawers in search for something that didn’t look ridiculously expensive. Sang-woo probably would have had something to say about the snooping if he wasn’t so out of it, but Gi-hun had a feeling that the man would go to sleep with jeans and shoes on if he didn’t find a comfier alternative.

“Bottom drawer,” Sang-woo finally got out before muffling a cough into his sleeve. The coughs continued as Gi-hun opened the correct drawer and found a pair of sweats and a T-shirt that looked to be at least a decade old.

“Aha, I knew you had some shitty clothes somewhere!”

Sang-woo snorted, and then coughed again.

Gi-hun gave him privacy to change while he went to fix his dinner. The apartment’s kitchen wasn’t big, but it was nicer than his own. Sang-woo wasn’t kidding, though. There was no food to be found. A lot of soju in the fridge, and some milk.

Some empty bottles next to the sink, and a few empty cigarette cartons as well, but there wasn’t nearly enough of anything for the place to actually look cluttered.

Gi-hun hadn’t realized how hungry he really was until he started to scarf the stuff down. It wasn’t tasty by any means, but his stomach didn’t care. He only remembered why he was there in the first place when he heard coughing from the other room. It was the same breathless cough that had Sang-woo nearly keeling over on their way there.

“Sang-woo?” he called, not having completely swallowed his food.

The coughs continued, and then the sound of frantic stumbling. Gi-hun shot up and opened the door to find his old friend seated on the ground. Sang-woo tried desperately to get a breath in, it didn’t work well.

“Shit, I...” Gi-hun trailed off as he went to grab a glass of water.

It wasn’t long before Sang-woo was nursing the water. His breath was still wheezy, and he still coughed at least every minute, but the water seemed to soothe some of the irritation. He was still on the ground, so Gi-hun sat down with him. Sang-woo didn’t look in his direction, kept his eyes on the water in his hands.

“You’re going to need help getting back into bed?” Gi-hun assumed.

A quick nod. “Probably.”

“When you’re ready, though.”

Sang-woo nodded again and took another sip of water. He shuddered, despite the red patches on his cheeks. Gi-hun wasn’t sure he’d seen him so miserable. Not before the games, at least.

A few minutes passed before Sang-woo let out a raspy breath, set his water on the nightstand he was leaned against, and started to pull himself to his feet. Gi-hun lent him a hand and held him steady as he slowly got onto the bed once again.

“Where were you going, anyway?”

Sang-woo glanced at his nightstand. “Water.”

“Oh,” he chuckled. “Well, there we go, then.”

“Mhm,” he sniffed, dragging the back of his hand across his nose. He shakily got himself under the blankets with Gi-hun’s help.

Gi-hun realized the bottom drawer of the dresser was still open, and looked messy after he’d dug through it looking for the absolute comfiest pajamas. He was always bad at cleaning up after himself. That was something that was going to have to change now. He started to tidy the clothes.

“Gi-hun.”

“Hmm?” He looked up. Sang-woo’s eyes had been closed, as if he’d fallen asleep, but they drifted open as Gi-hun went back to him attentively. Maybe he was hungry, or he needed help with something else. Or maybe he just wanted Gi-hun to stop ruining his organizational system for his clothes.

“Why are you here?”

“What?”

“Why are you bothering with me?”

Gi-hun frowned. “What do you mean?”

“You know what I mean,” Sang-woo said, expression surprisingly sober for someone burning up with fever. “I’m not worth all this. You should just leave me to - how did you say it? - drown.”

Drown on their own lungs was the exact phrasing Gi-hun had used, but he had a feeling that that wasn’t what Sang-woo meant.

He wanted Gi-hun to give up on him.

Gi-hun wanted to give up on him.

“I can’t,” Gi-hun said simply. “I just can’t.” It was an admission to himself as much as it was to Sang-woo.

Sang-woo gave a minute nod, and a few seconds of heavy silence passed.

“I don't regret any of it.”

Gi-hun clenched his jaw. It was nothing he didn’t already know, but the reminder still made him feel sick.

“You should hate me.” Sang-woo wheezed, his breath becoming just a bit quicker.

Gi-hun swallowed and looked at him. “Do you hate me?”

Sang-woo turned to fully face him. “I don’t think that I could if I tried.”

He took a deep breath, and then another, to try to calm his racing heart. Tears blurred his vision. This was the conversation he’d dreaded.

Gi-hun found himself resting a hand on the man’s forehead, brushing his hair aside as he did so. He grabbed the plastic bag from the other side of the bed and started rummaging through it uselessly.

“Are any of these a fever reducer?” He asked, voice shaking. “You... you’ll need some if you’re going to get any sleep, probably.”

Sang-woo continued to look at him like he had the answer to everything. He didn’t. Gi-hun didn’t have a single answer for anything that had happened to them. He wasn’t equipped to handle any of this, never was.

“White bottle, I think,” Sang-woo rasped finally.

Gi-hun nodded and opened the seal of the bottle. He shook out two pills and handed them to Sang-woo as the man pulled himself up against the wall. Sang-woo took the water from his nightstand and swallowed them easily.

“Thank you,” he said.

Gi-hun didn’t respond. He didn’t trust himself not to sob if he opened his mouth again.

“You truly want to stay the night?”

He nodded again. “I think it’d be best.”

“Well... this bed has always been too big for just me. If- if you didn’t want to deal with that terrible sofa. Although, I guess I might keep you up.”

It wouldn’t have been the first time they’d shared a bed. There was the hotel on their school trip to a science competition, and the many times that they had sleepovers as kids.

Gi-hun climbed on the other side with a watery chuckle. “Like old times, huh?”

“In a way,” he said with a smile fonder than he had any right to wear.

Sang-woo didn’t fall asleep easily at first. It took a while for the medicine to take effect, and even when the shivers were gone, the cough wasn’t.

Gi-hun helped him stack the pillows in a way that would be comfortable for him to sleep upright, and fixed his blankets for him when they got tangled and wrapped in his legs.

Soon, Sang-woo’s body fell into the rest it desperately needed.

It was earlier than Gi-hun was used to going to bed, but he found that he was pretty worn out. From what, he had no idea.

It wasn’t long before he succumbed to sleep himself, all while wondering what horrors he would dream about this time.
