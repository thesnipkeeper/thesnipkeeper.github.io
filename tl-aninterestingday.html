An Interesting Day

Ted couldn’t help but be delighted when he got the email from his good old reporter friend. Trent had requested an interview. He, of course, signed said email with Trent Crimm, The Independent.

Ted waited for the day to come. Like a kid waiting to go to Disneyland, except instead of a cartoon mouse, it was a handsome man with reading glasses and misleading questions.

Their back and forth was almost part of the game at this point. Ted prided himself on being frank and honest and easy to talk to. Trent, on the other hand, wanted a story, and just happened to be very polite while he rummaged through a person’s mental trash. (that last part was a quote from Rebecca, who apparently had an unfortunate history with the man’s reporting).

Ted was jittery with excitement as the team made their way out to the pitch. Trent was going to join them any time now, and he and Ted would get to chat all they wanted while he coached.

He checked his watch. Trent Crimm, The Independent, was ten minutes late.

“Strange,” said Beard, as if he could read Ted’s mind. He probably could, if Ted was being honest with himself. “Never pegged him as a late person.”

“Agreed,” Ted said with an impatient huff. He didn’t want to be dramatic, but his whole day would be ruined if his buddy Trent didn’t show up.

“I’m sure he’ll be here.” Beard was absolutely a mind reader.

“Hope so!”

And as if on cue, a handsome long-haired gentleman made his way onto the pitch with a wave.

Ted waved back enthusiastically. “Thought we lost ya!”

“Oh no, never,” Trent cleared his throat. “I’m very sorry, there was a... situation at home, and-” he shook his head. “No, that’s unprofessional of me to talk about. Sorry I’m late, Coach Lasso.”

“No prob, Bob. Hope everything’s okay!”

“It is,” he said, but he looked a bit too frazzled to be telling the truth.

He flipped to a page on his notepad and started asking questions like there was no tomorrow. And this was the part Ted couldn’t get enough of. He’d had his fair share of dumb interview questions. He knew when he was talking to a good reporter.

Trent’s questions were good, but Trent seemed... distracted. He fumbled through sentences and paused at weird moments and Ted couldn’t help but take notice. This wasn’t the Trent Crimm he knew.

“What do you hope for the team to accomplish in this next...” Trent stopped. He closed his eyes and tilted his head up just slightly, as if he were in pain and waiting for it to pass.

Ted immediately frowned. “You okay?”

Trent nodded, but he didn’t look okay. He opened his eyes once more and exhaled. “Apologies, I-” his hand - still holding his fancy reporter pen - went to his mouth, fingers laying over his lips. He swallowed.

Ted could identify that look a mile away. “Oh boy... Trent, y'alright?"

Trent nodded, paused, and shook his head.

"... you gonna puke?"

He nodded. The poor man had given up on trying to talk. He looked like he was trying desperately not to blow chunks all over the field.

“Yeah, that’s kinda what I figured. C’mon...” Ted laid a hand on his back as the two made their way back inside the club. Trent moved briskly for someone who was usually so composed, hand staying close to but not over his mouth, like he was prepared for any unfortunate incident.

He darted into the bathroom as soon as he could. Ted moved to follow him in, and thought better of it. Probably best not to invade his privacy while he was barfing.

So, he found himself standing in the hallway awkwardly, with his hands in his pockets. He nodded at each person who walked by as if he belonged there. What a weird day this was turning out to be.

Should he go back out to the pitch, or...?

Trent emerged looking like death warmed over. His hair was tied back now, though not very neatly, and he had his jacket draped over his arm like it was far too hot for him right now. He didn’t seem the least bit surprised to find Ted still in the hallway, though he did look pretty embarrassed.

“Apologies,” his voice was tight and raspy compared to the usual smooth tone Ted loved so much. “Jesus... I’m sorry, Ted. I don’t-”

Ted couldn’t help but frown in sympathy. “Hey,” he squeezed Trent’s shoulder. “Don’t apologize! It happens! You feeling any better after that?”

“I’m... not sure,” he admitted. He didn’t look much better.

“Maybe it was just an upset stomach? You eat leftover pizza for breakfast? That never has great results."

Trent swallowed hard and shook his head at the thought. "Emily's had a bit of a bug…" he sighed out.

He didn’t talk about his daughter much, definitely not when he was working. Ted admired his ability to keep his home life and work life separate and also wondered how the hell a guy could manage not to talk about his kid all the time. Trent was a disciplined individual. Ted didn’t even know he had a kid until he ended up asking one day out of the need for small talk.

"Ah, yeah, that'll do it." Ted chuckled. "Kids, gotta love'em."

“I really, really thought that I’d escaped it.”

“Look, why don’t we do this some other time? You should be home resting and all that.”

Trent looked like that was all he’d wanted to do since he arrived, but he shook his head. “If you don’t mind, I really need to get this done. Stomach bug or no, I still have a deadline.”

“Alright, of course,” Ted shoved his hands in his pocket and nodded, offering a pleasant smile. “But why don’t we do this in my office? No sense in standing out in the cold.”

He smiled and nodded, breathing out in relief. “That’s fine with me, Coach Lasso.”
