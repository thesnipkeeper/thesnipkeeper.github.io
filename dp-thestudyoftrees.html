The Study of Trees

“Is he feeling any better?” Rayla asked quietly as she walked in, closing the door behind her with care.

“I think so,” he said with a tired sigh. He desperately needed a nap after the long day, but he couldn’t. Not just yet. “His fever broke a few hours ago.”

“That’s good, right?”

Ethari made an unsure noise. “It could come back, but for now he’s just drained.”

Rayla frowned and looked at him for longer than he would have liked. He turned uncomfortable under her scrutiny, knowing how obvious it must have been.

“What about you? How are you doin’?”

Ethari opened his mouth, fully prepared to lie and tell her that he was fine. But the no-nonsense look on her face, far too much like Runaan when he knew Ethari was about to say he was fine when he wasn’t, had him admitting the truth.

“I’m tired,” he sighed out, shoulders falling. “I don’t want to sleep until I know he’s okay.”

“He’s an assassin. He’ll be fine.” She was far too mature for a fifteen year-old, Ethari decided. To have his child, or rather, the child he was raising, comfort him felt both warm and a little embarrassing that he’d needed it. It wasn’t her job to check on him, on either of them, and yet Rayla did just as much to take care of them as they did her these days.

And she was right.

Runaan was an assassin, he’d gotten through much worse with no problem. Ethari recalled a time when his husband returned from a mission looking slightly more exhausted than usual, only for him to admit that he’d accomplished said mission with a high fever. To imagine his husband working as he always did while that sick was terrifying, but it did assure him that he didn’t always need to worry about Runaan.

Just because he didn’t need to worry didn’t mean he never did, though.

“Are you getting sick too?” Rayla asked, reaching up to feel his forehead. He leaned down for easier access for her, even though he knew that he wasn’t sick, just tired from caring for a sick husband all day on three hours of sleep. She bit the inside of her lip and retracted her hand. “You’re not warm.”

Ethari smiled. “I’m fine, love. Do you have schoolwork you should be doing?”

Rayla huffed, blowing a piece of hair out of her face. “I mean, we’re supposed to be studying trees or somethin’, but it’s so boring, Ethari. Can’t I do it later?”

Ethari chuckled. “That does sound boring,” he admitted. “Alright, then, go... wait, Rayla, what are you-?” he suddenly found himself being led to the bedroom, Rayla taking his hand in hers and nearly dragging him along behind her.

Even as quiet as they were as they entered, Runaan’s eyes still opened. He’d always been a light sleeper. He had to be, to be alert on his missions. As opposed to Ethari, who could sleep through the end of the world with no problem.

Runaan smiled at him, and then at Rayla. “Hello. What’s going on?” his voice was rough with sleep, words slurring together as they had been most of the day.

“Look at Ethari. He looks like he’s about to pass out or somethin’. I’m forcing him to nap.” Rayla said, gesturing toward him. Immediately throwing him under the bridge, huh? What a betrayal.

Ethari didn’t bother to fight it as she (somewhat gently) shoved him into bed next to his husband. Runaan laid a hand on his chest as if to keep him from getting up again.

“She’s right, you look as awful as I do,” Runaan said with a chuckle.

Ethari couldn’t help but smile. It was nice to be in bed, he had to admit, but if he was sleeping, then who would-?

“Tell me if you need anything, yeah?” Rayla’s voice had softened significantly.

“Rayla...” Ethari tried, but she shook her head.

“No, you both need sleep. I can take care of everything.”

Ethari and Runaan had already gravitated toward each other. Ethari could feel sleep trying to claim him already. After an early morning of checking his husband’s fever and administering medicine, his body was ready for a rest.

“Thank you, Rayla,” Ethari mumbled, eyes slipping shut.

“No problem. Get some rest.”

-----

It was no surprise that Ethari woke the next morning with a fever of his own. He and Runaan weren’t exactly skilled at not sharing illness. Something that Rayla constantly enjoyed pointing out was the fact that the two of them were always sick at the same time. They tried to deny it the first time she said it, but they realized that it was absolutely true.

Runaan insisted that it was his turn to feel his forehead and help him feel better.

“You’re still sick, love,” Ethari said, reaching up to cup Runaan’s cheek. “You shouldn’t be out of bed, not yet.”

Runaan smiled and laid his own hand over Ethari’s before turning to kiss his palm. “I’ll be just fine. Not to mention, Rayla was about to stay home from class because she was so worried.”

Ethari huffed out a tired laugh. “May have somethin’ to do with the fact that they’re studying trees this week.”

“Ah,” Runaan nodded. “That makes sense. Agriculture isn’t exactly her favorite subject, is it?”

Ethari went to sit up, but found himself to be just a little too lightheaded and weak to do so. Thankfully Runaan realized what he was trying to do and helped him with what little strength he probably had.

“What do you need, my love? I can get you some food, if you’d like?”

Ethari shook his head. The thought of eating made his stomach tighten, and he wasn’t about to make the man who was completely bedridden just the day before to fetch him breakfast.

He took Runaan’s hand in his own. “I’d like you to come back to bed.”

Runaan frowned. “You waited on me hand and foot yesterday. The least I could do for you is get you some water, or medicine...”

“I just want you, Runaan. Please. Would it help if I said that I was chilled?” He grinned.

Runaan gave a fond sigh, gently taking his hand out of Ethari’s grip and standing up off of the edge of the bed. He moved to the other side slowly, a clear sign that he still wasn’t feeling himself. Ethari made a delighted noise as Runaan wrapped an arm around him and pressed a kiss to his temple.

“Get some rest, dear heart.”

“I plan to,” Ethari replied. Strangely, despite the fact that they were both ill, both relatively miserable, and were probably going to have to rely on their teenager for the day...

It was nice.
