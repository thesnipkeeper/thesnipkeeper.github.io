Title: How It Used To Be
Fandom: Encanto
Summary: Bruno and the rest of the family learn that things have changed since they were kids
Word count: 3.7k

How It Used To Be

Bruno was the smallest of the triplets. He always thought it unfair, seeing as though he was the boy. It didn’t seem right that his sisters continued to tower over him while their mother promised that a growth spurt was on its way any day now. That growth spurt never arrived, of course.

Not only was he the smallest, but the weakest. 

And the one who got sick twice as much as his sisters.

Julieta was the carer more often than not. Her cooking may only have been able to do so much for illness, but she still had the nature about her. Pepa’s care was more concealed under scoffs and eye rolls (“You’re ill again? Honestly, Bruno!). She was still the one to stay up with him late at night when he couldn’t sleep and Julieta was exhausted from taking care of him all day.

Things were different when he left. 

He didn’t get sick nearly as often when he was hidden in the walls, at least not traditionally so, which was nice. Random fevers came and went on occasion, and he’d wrap himself up on his sad little cot until he wasn’t shivering anymore. Sometimes that took hours, sometimes it took days. Whatever, he lived. He needed to learn to take care of himself, to be alone. He forced himself to learn.

So Bruno was prepared to do the same thing when he woke with a headache and the inability to sit up without the room spinning. The strength had been sapped from his body, leaving him a limp blob of ugh while he tried to decide whether he was hot or cold.

A faint tinge of nausea nagged him when he turned over on his side, as if making promises for things to come.

Magical windows that didn’t have a true place on the outside showed him that the sun was further than it usually was. Bruno could hear the muffled voices of his family, plates and glasses clinking as they got ready for breakfast.

He’d slept in.

He tried to sit up once more, and darkness quickly crawled along the edges of his vision. His stomach threatened to bring up last night’s dinner. Nope, not happening.

Well, that sucked.

He huffed at his situation and lowered himself back into the warmth of his bedding. His room was usually the perfect temperature for him, but today it was too cold.

Bruno’s tower had fixed itself since he returned. Readjusted and rearranged until it was less of a horrifying fortress and more of a cool room that doubled as a giant sandbox. He thought of it as Casita’s way of welcoming him back from the misery of exile.

He moaned. He’d hardly moved and his body already ached like he ran a marathon.

He listened to his family have breakfast without him. Just another family meal that he missed to add to the thousands of others, Bruno supposed.

He fell asleep.

-----

“He’s asleep, mamá,” was the first thing Bruno heard, the hushed voice of his dear sister Julieta.

He didn’t open his eyes, that was too much work, apparently. Since when did sleep make you more tired than you already were?

Bruno didn’t even have the energy to flinch when a cold hand landed on his forehead.

He heard his mother mutter something of concern under her breath before giving a resigned and informing, “He’s burning up...” there was something different in the tone of her voice than there used to be when his mother learned of his fevers.

She’d always tried to hide her disappointment whenever Julieta or Pepa came to her, telling her of Bruno’s most recent illness. He had things to do for the family, after all, and how could he do any of it when he was bedridden more often than not?

But this time his mother’s hand lingered on his skin as he heard her sigh deeply. “I suppose it was bound to happen eventually, now that he’s back,” she said, sounding almost sad instead of accusatory.

Julieta hummed. “I can look after him, Pepa and I both. Don’t you worry, mamá. It’ll be just like the old days.”

There was a moment of silence before Alma sighed once again. “I’ll... be back later to make sure everything’s alright.”

There were footsteps before the door closed.

Julieta sighed.

Bruno’s plan after that was to fall asleep once again and hopefully stay asleep for the next week or so until his body didn’t hate him anymore. Perhaps it was because of the hate his body harbored for him that he couldn’t go back to sleep and instead found himself incredibly achy and uncomfortable. His brows pinched together as he shifted to try and find a position that didn’t make his muscles cry out in pain. It was no use.

Bruno forced himself to give up on sleep and open his eyes.

Julieta was in the process of wringing out a cloth over a bowl of water. There was a bowl of soup on a tray on top of his chest of drawers (which was empty, he really needed to upgrade his wardrobe to more than a couple changes of clothes).

“I missed breakfast,” he slurred. His voice came out weak and raspy from not having spoken all day.

Julieta looked up, and immediately chuckled. “You missed breakfast, and also lunch.” He should really feel hungry then, huh? Except he really did not. “We were trying to give you space until Delores said that your breath sounded strange.”

Space. All he’d had was space for the past decade, and yet he found himself craving isolation on bad days. He was so thankful to have his family back, but they realized even before he had that sometimes he still needed space.

“It’s not that bad, sis...” he tried. “I’m a grown man, you don’t have to stick around all day just ‘cause of some bug.”

“Right right,” Julieta nodded. She sat on the edge of the bed and began gently dabbing his forehead and temple with the cool cloth. He gasped at first, and then found that it brought a bit of relief. “Because if I left you here alone right now, you would take care of yourself? And eat and drink and rest?”

“Fair point,” he huffed. “But it’s not like that’s your job.”

“It truly is my job, Bruno.”

“Another fair point.”

“Thank you,” Julieta chuckled and draped the cloth on the edge of the bowl, setting it aside. “You should eat something today, though. It’ll make you feel better.” She was already grabbing the tray before he could answer.

Coming from anyone else, Bruno wouldn’t believe that for a second. His sister’s cooking, on the other hand, he had faith in. Maybe it would ease the aches and allow him to move without wanting to cry.

He did his best to sit up. It... took longer than it should for any normal human to sit up in bed. He paused halfway up with his eyes squeezed shut as dizziness tried to take over once again.

“Easy...” she cautioned.

Julieta waited patiently before she set the tray in his lap.

It looked like amazing soup, and likely smelled just as amazing. Was that the same recipe she used to make for the family whenever they were sick? The familiarity warmed him.

Bruno had absolutely no appetite. Still, he began to eat.

At first he thought it was helping. Maybe Julieta was right, maybe he just needed some food in his system. His achy muscles eased after the very first spoonful, and he started to have faith that he’d be okay after this.

He was halfway through the bowl when he realized that the soup had somehow formed a rock in his stomach, sitting heavy. Bruno was reminded of the vague nausea that had been nagging him. Although, it wasn’t vague anymore.

“Bruno? Everything okay?”

He’d stopped eating, a hand hovering in front of his mouth as he swallowed the last spoonful of soup that he’d managed to get. He sat still for fear of making anything worse.

“Y-yeah, I just-”

And that last spoonful he’d managed to swallow was the first to come up as he gagged into his hand and leaned limply over the edge of his bed. He wasn’t sure how Julieta had managed to get a basin that quickly, but he was glad to not have vomited allover the floor or his sister. He wanted to keep some of his dignity. He took the basin into his own grip and continued to heave painfully.

She was quick to pull his hair out of the line of fire - they’d done this plenty of times before - and run a hand up and down his back.

“Deep breaths,” Julieta instructed. She did a poor job of hiding her worry. She’d spent a good amount of time telling him to put on some weight since he’d come back. It was probably disappointing to see all of her progress go to waste before her.

It took a few tries before he was able to exhale without heaving up his stomach contents. He breathed out again. Was it over?

Julieta let go of his hair and pulled a falling piece behind his ear as he caught his breath and spat into the bowl below.

“Sorry about the soup,” was the first thing he thought to say, with a tired chuckle as he wiped his mouth with his sleeve. “It did help, I promise.”

Julieta laughed and ran a hand through his messy curls before she took the thing from his grasp. He was too tired to argue. Jeez he was tired. “Don’t you worry about it,” but she was, he could see it in her eyes. Always worried about her brother no matter how old they were. 

-----

He heard the conversation at his bedside. He probably wasn’t supposed to, but then why would they be saying those things right there when he was trying to sleep? Rude.

“Mamá, please...”

“... has a responsibility to this family no matter the state...”

“... ‘s barely conscious. There’s no way he can-”

“That’s his decision.”

“Is it?!”

There was a pause as his mother took a deep breath. “The two of you must understand that you all have gifts for a reason.”

“We understand that, mamá, we just don’t want to see him get hurt,” came Julieta’s voice, gentle and cautious.

“-Hurt because you decided to torture him through another vision!” and Pepa’s less gentle voice. Bruno felt some stray raindrops on his face.

Bruno had been bedridden for three days. He’d come down with the flu that had been going around the village, and it hit him harder than it’d hit either of his sisters the week before. They’d been with him the entire time as his fever broke and then returned over and over again to make sure he was as miserable as possible.

“This is important for the village,” said Alma.

“I can do it,” he said as he finally opened his eyes. “Really, I can.”

His mother smiled while Pepa and Julieta frowned.

Bruno found himself waking fully, sitting up in bed with the help of Pepa who was trying to avoid raining on her sick brother. Julieta’s hand was on his forehead for a split second before she sighed sadly and shook her head in silent disapproval.

He made his slow way to his tower. He set up his things, he got the vision the village wouldn’t want. An earthquake was on its way, and he’d be responsible for the news once again.

The vision faded along with the green in his eyes.

And he collapsed.

His family would find him burning up with fever, and he’d be bedridden for another week.

-----

The first thing that Bruno did when he woke and found himself alone was try to get up. He swung his legs over the edge of the bed and was about to stand when he realized that his vision would go black if he didn’t stop moving for two seconds.

There were voices downstairs. It was breakfast, again. He slept through the whole night.

Well, sort of. He had vague memories of his sister - Pepa, not Julieta this time - dabbing his forehead with a cool cloth and helping him sit up when he started to feel sick again.

Bruno smiled at that. And then frowned when he remembered that she had a family now, they both did. Children and spouses who needed them far more than he did.

They weren’t kids anymore.

A rush of guilt ran through him. He was far, far too old to need anyone to take care of him. He hadn’t needed it for a decade, after all. It was unfair to rely on them so heavily the moment that they welcome him back.

What if it became too much? What if he was forced to exile himself once again?

He felt- he didn’t feel well. Actually, he really didn’t feel well.

Bruno found himself grabbing the conveniently-placed basin and getting it in his lap just in time to catch a trickle of vomit as he coughed it up. He spat, tried to breathe.

It didn’t feel like anything else was in danger of coming up. Apparently his stomach was done, it just needed a single insult to make him more miserable than he already did. Bruno ran his sleeve across his face and set the thing aside.

Voices sounded from breakfast. He heard his name in a conversation.

Bruno grabbed hold of the bed frame and slowly pulled himself to his feet. All he had to do was stand, was that so hard? Couldn’t his legs cooperate?

Nope.

He lowered himself back onto his bed and let his breath and heartbeat settle from such a strenuous venture.

This was stupid.

He stood again, without the help of the furniture or anything (anyone) else.

The room spun at a rapid speed while darkness slowly creeped into his vision once again. He’d taken two steps before he heard the door open and-

“Hijo!”

Mamá?

He fell to the ground. He faded out, succumbing to sleep or something like it until the thumping of rapid footsteps woke him again.

Several voices surrounded him, crowded him. Someone - ah, Luisa - scooped him up with little effort and gently laid him on his bed. Someone’s hand on his cheek, his forehead, brushing his tangled hair away and muttering under her breath.

He’d never heard his mother swear before, but he was sure she had.

Bruno let his eyes open to find half of his family in his room, all with equally-concerned expressions on their faces.

“I...”

“What,” Pepa was the first person to say something while her husband left and shooed the kids out with him. “Were you doing out of bed?”

That was a good question. “I was... coming down for breakfast?”

It started to rain, just slightly as his sister stared him down with the same look that terrified him since they were kids.

Julieta sighed.

Their mother continued to stare. Bruno felt uncomfortable under the worried look she was giving him. Maybe he did something wrong. No, he’d definitely done something wrong if he ended up passed out on the floor.

“Mamá was bringing breakfast to you.”

Bruno turned to where Pepa pointed and saw that there was a tray on his bureau that had clearly been thrown down in haste. He pressed his lips into a thin line, lacking anything to say until,

“Ah.”

Alma shook her head. “You’re too ill for- what possessed you to get out of bed and-” she huffed. “If you had fainted on the stairs, I-”

“Casita wouldn’t have let that happen,” Julieta assured their mother with a hand on her arm.

But she was right. He got so wrapped up in not wanting to be a bother that he made himself even more of a bother. What would have happened if he’d hurt himself? They would have had to take care of him allover again. Even now, he was disrupting their day when there were so many other people that needed help.

He felt his mother’s hand cup his face before he realized that there were tears rolling down his cheek.

Ah, crap.

“Give us a moment,” she said with a tightness in her voice, and didn’t bother to turn away from him. She didn’t need to, Bruno’s sisters left the room without a word.

Bruno was in the process of trying to dry up his face, trying to sit up in bed and look less pathetic. Alma sat on the edge, her hand slowly leaving his face. She wore an expression that was undecipherable besides sadness. What did he do this time?

“I’ve been too hard on you,” it was almost a whisper.

“Mamá-”

She cut him off with a shake of her head. “Mirabel, your sisters, they’ve helped me realize that I expected too much from you as children.”

His mother looked like she was about to cry, so naturally he tried to lighten the mood with a chuckle and a, “All I did was get out of bed.”

“Yes, and why?”

Because... because he wanted to prove that he wasn’t useless. But he couldn’t say that, so instead, “I...”

She kissed his forehead, cupped his cheek one more time, and stood up to get the abandoned tray.

“You still need to eat.”

He really wasn’t hungry, especially after passing out on the floor. But he still took a bite, and then another. The room was filled with an odd silence as he did so, as if something was still hanged in the air.

He thanked her after not even half the food was gone. He couldn’t eat any more and be sure that he wouldn’t vomit it up. He was also... exhausted. Tired. Tired of being awake, tired of... having to think about their weird family and the way his mother was looking at him, as if she’d ruined him.

“Thank you, Mamá,” he said softly. “I- I think I’ll go back to sleep.”

She smiled and nodded. “Good. Get some rest.”

He planned on it.

-----

The next time he woke, it was to shattering glass and a “Shoot!”.

Bruno found himself sitting up the moment he was awake, as if he were sick of sleeping. “Mirabel,” he greeted with a sleepy chuckle as he watched his niece try to scoop up broken glass with a towel.

She looked at him with wide eyes. “Tío Bruno! I’m so sorry, I was trying to be quiet, but-”

He shook his head and rubbed the sleep from his eyes. “I probably needed to wake up anyway.” She went to pick up a big, sharp piece with her bare hand and he made a noise to stop her. “Ay! Be careful with broken glass!” he said with an exasperated chuckle.

She retracted her hand and sighed, hands falling to her lap as she sat on the floor. “Sorry, sorry...”

“What was- Ah,” Julieta entered, saw the broken glass, and connected it to her daughter immediately. “I’ll get the broom.” but before leaving, she turned to him. “Feeling better?”

“I- I think so?” He said. “Sure, yeah.”

She smiled. “Good.”

He listened to Mirabel apologize for the thousandth time as her mother dismissed each one with a dismissive laugh or a kiss to the temple.

He never really saw it before he’d already left. The way that Mirabel was raised, believing that she was unimportant because she didn’t have a gift. Maybe his mother was right, maybe she really had screwed them all up a little bit.

But, he thought, that was sort of a mother’s job. It was hard to blame her.

Mirabel left with the broken glass shards while Julieta took what hadn’t tipped off the tray. This time Bruno didn’t argue as it was handed to him. He began to eat rather easily. Maybe he was on the mend.

“She said something strange this morning.”

“Mamá?”

He nodded. “Something about being too hard on us. It was- I mean, it was really weird.”

She frowned and thought for a moment. “I think that she’s trying to make up for the-” she tried to think of a better way to put it, but settled on the more scathing word, “-damage that’s been done.”

“Ah psh,” Bruno waved it off. “It wasn’t that bad. She loved us, and she was doing what she thought was right.”

“That’s true, that’s true,” Julieta said with a nod. “But you still left for a decade because you were afraid of disappointing her. And- and the rest of us.” Her eyes watered a little. Bruno really wished all this crying stuff would stop, he wasn’t a fan.

He swallowed the lump in his throat. “Yeah,” he admitted quietly. “You’re not wrong.”

“Let her do what she thinks she must do.”

“Can I ask you a question, then?”

Julieta looked expectantly.

“Are you and Pepa doing this because you think you need to?”

Julieta’s jaw set for just a moment. It was the same look Pepa often had before exploding, but Julieta’s frustration was much more subtle than that.

“We’re doing this because you’re family, and that’s what family does. Yes, maybe there’s a need to make up for lost time, but because we love you. Not because we feel obligated. I think you know that we’ll all continue to take care of each other until we can’t anymore.”

Bruno sighed. “I’m not your burden.”

“You’re not anyone’s burden. You’re our brother.”

Bruno felt unconvinced. All that time away made him a more... cautious (paranoid) person.

“Bruno, you’ve done the same for us plenty of times, for our children as well. For Mamá. You really think that we’re not going to do the same for you?”

He chuckled. “The score is a little unbalanced when it’s always me who’s sick or injured.”

“Yes, well,” Julieta snorted. “Good thing that score is in your imagination and no one else’s. Eat your food.”

He did.

Pepa joined them later that day. They talked and joked and laughed like they were kids again and not grown adults.

Alma joined them not too long after.

Just the four of them together again, but this time there was no concern for whether they were good enough for their mother, perfect enough, using their gifts exactly the way she wanted them to.

Just family. They were just a family.
