Before and After

Owen glanced at his list for the tenth time. Bananas, oatmeal, milk, eggs, coffee, coffee filters, BBQ chips (he was on a BBQ chip kick, alright?), some frozen dinners wouldn’t hurt later, though he didn’t add it to his list (perhaps it was too sad and lonely to include).

The grocery store was quiet, no people to weave through or running children to avoid. He loved shopping at this time of night, he couldn’t help it. Everything was quiet and calm and he could just get his things and go home without waiting in line. Joan always teased him for it, but she accompanied him almost every time.

He was content.

Bananas, his first stop. They were at the front of the store, after all. The pile had yet to be restocked but he never minded.

The usual hum in the store gave way to a sudden sneeze from a few aisles away. It sounded... familiar; he pushed it away as feeling nostalgic late at night, and told himself that he needed to get a grip.

Oatmeal was next, and he decided that he may as well grab a box of granola bars to keep in his office while he was there. Chocolate chip, of course.

Another sneeze from the same person who must’ve been in the next aisle over. This time, however, it was followed by a miserable nose blow. He winced in sympathy. Whoever it was clearly wasn’t feeling well.

Next was the coffee stuff, in that same aisle. He wandered over, already trying to decide what flavor he wanted. Hazelnut was good, but maybe it was time for a change. French vanilla? Or one of those fancy dessert-flavored grounds. Yes, it was settled. The first chocolate flavored coffee he laid his eyes on would be his.

Another sneeze just as he rounded the corner with his basket. He was so focused on where the flavored coffees were that he didn’t bother to look up until he noticed the other person was facing him.

“Joan!” He could’ve sounded less happier to see her, but he was never good at pretending with her. “Sorry, uh, Dr. Bright.”

She stared wide eyed, confused, the bottom half of her face still covered with a tissue under her steepled hands. She slowly lowered it, the confused look remaining. “Agent Green,” her voice broke in the middle and she had to clear her throat. Owen winced. “What um, what are you doing here at ten at night?”

“Well, this is when I do my grocery shopping, you know that.”

Joan nodded. “Right, that’s right.” She sniffled and dabbed at her nose with the same tissue. By the box in her basket, it seemed to be her last one.

She looked so tired and sick. The skin around her nose was red and chapped, and her eyes were watery. Her hair had been lazily pulled into a bun which looked fine to others, of course, but she wouldn’t have usually considered wearing in public, because that was the kind of person that Joan was.

“Dr. Bright, you... you look terrible.” It came out before he could stop it. He was never good at saying the right thing to her after- well, after.

Joan immediately glared. “Wow, thank you, Agent Green. So nice of you t-”

“No, no I didn’t mean- you just...” he sighed, his shoulders falling. “Sorry.”

Her lips formed a thin line, eyes narrowing as if to think of what to say to him next. It wasn’t going to be kind, he was sure of that. Instead of speaking, however, her breath hitched suddenly and she rushed to cover a sneeze with the same abused tissue in her hand. The involuntary groan that followed indicated either an exacerbated sore throat or headache from the sneeze.

Owen frowned. His heart hurt. He wanted to do something, to take her home and wrap her in blankets and make her tea and-

No, he couldn’t think like that, not anymore. Those times were long over. Joan... Joan hated him. And despite his defending himself during every argument, he was starting to understand why.

“Bless you, Dr. Bright.”

Joan averted her eyes as she cleaned up her nose. She sniffled wetly, the sneeze having apparently made things worse. He averted his eyes as well to save her from embarrassment, absently glancing at her basket instead.

Cold medicine (which was odd, she always claimed it did nothing and refused to take it. She must’ve been feeling awful if she was resorting to that), orange juice (“Joan, it'll hurt your throat!” “I’ll be fine, I just want some vitamin C”), vitamins (of course.), hand sanitizer (also, of course), a thermometer (she didn’t already have one of those? Really? Right... she usually spent her sick days at his place.) The last thing he noticed was the tea that he always made for her when she wasn’t feeling her best. For some reason, that tea in her shopping basket was the biggest punch to the gut he’d experienced yet since running into the woman he still loved.

She was miserable, and he could do nothing about it.

She shoved the tissue in the pocket of her sweatpants (since when did Joan wear sweats outside her home?), but she was still sniffling desperately with a runny nose that didn’t want to quit.

He had to help. Somehow. Before seeing her like this broke his heart completely. He didn’t even finish that thought before a light bulb dinged inside his head and he fished the folded kerchief from his breast pocket.

“Here, you seem like you need this.”

She stared at the proffered handkerchief as if it were some gesture to figure out. It wasn’t, he was done trying that, trying to trick her into liking him again. He just wanted to help.

Joan scoffed, and for the first time since he’d seen her, the smallest smile tugged at her lips as she accepted it. “I can’t believe you still carry handkerchiefs around with you.” She turned away slightly to dab at her nose, and he helpfully averted his gaze for a moment.

“It never hurts to be prepared.”

“I suppose not.” She sniffed experimentally. “Um, thank you, Owen. I’ll... return this at our next quarterly review.”

“Don’t worry about it,” he cleared his throat.

She nodded, cleared her own throat, though out of necessity. She pressed the kerchief to her nose again and looked around as if to make sure no one else was watching.

“So,” he turned to the coffee as he spoke. He often thought it was rude not to look at someone when you were talking to them, but in this case he felt that she preferred it. “You’re doing some late night shopping as well, hm?”

Joan didn’t answer. He waited, heart suddenly thumping as he wondered if he’d said something wrong. When he turned around, however, she had the kerchief over the lower half of her face with that distant about-to-sneeze look. That one sounded painful. Owen turned away and picked out his coffee (black forest cake flavored? Yes please). He tossed it in his basket in an attempt to seem casual, but he was never good at pretending to be casual, was he?

“Bless you!”

Joan ignored the blessing. “Well, snf! I didn’t think that I was getting much sleep tonight anyway, so I figured it was best to pick some things up before this,” she gestured vaguely to herself. “Got worse. Guess I was too late.”

He frowned. He wanted to keep talking to her, but... there was nothing else to say. She needed to go home and rest anyway, right? No use keeping her from that.

“I hope you feel better, Dr. Bright,” he said quietly, softly, with as much sincerity as he could muster. “Take care of that cold?”

“Thank you,” she said after a few moments. “I will. See you in a few weeks, I suppose.”

He nodded. “Two weeks and three days,” oh, for goodness' sake, as if that was important right now...

“Of course.” Joan muffled a cough into the kerchief as the two of them parted ways.

Owen went on to finish his grocery list with the knowledge that he was going to spend the rest of the night wishing that things were still the same as before.

And the knowledge that they never would be.
