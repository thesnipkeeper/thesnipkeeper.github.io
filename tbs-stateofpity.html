State of Pity

Joan hated being sick.

Of course, everyone hated being sick. But she didn’t just hate it because her nose kept running, or because her voice made her sound like a chain smoker, or even because she couldn’t sleep last night when she wasn’t sure if she was going to throw up or not (the answer was ‘or not’, but not after a good while of squirming in bed and trying to ignore the weird nausea).

No, she hated being sick because her brain stopped working. Joan unabashedly prided herself on her intelligence, her ability to think quicker than anyone else. She and her academic mind were buds. Just not today.

She got out of bed that morning with the intention of forcing herself to eat breakfast and a glass of juice, and instead flopped onto the couch.

She couldn’t think to save her life. Every time she tried, the thought would escape from her head as if it had nowhere to go, and she was left pathetically curled up on her couch, unable to think or move or be useful in any way.

Her nose was running. It never really stopped running. Joan sniffed and dragged her sleeve under her nose, something she’d have chided anyone else for doing.

She should make breakfast. Or go back to bed.

Water... when was the last time she drank water? What if she got dehydrated in her sleep?

... did that happen? She should know this...

Joan allowed herself a pitiful whimper as she curled up tighter underneath her thin throw blanket. This was... yeah, this was awful.

Knock knock.

The last thing that she wanted to do was get up from her warm nest and open the door, but she didn’t get visitors often and her curiosity got the best of her.

She dragged herself the mere few feet to the door, abandoning her blanket on the sofa and fixing her hair as she did so. There was no way of improving her appearance today.

“Sam?”

Sam smiled sheepishly. “Hey, hi Joan.”

Joan gasped. “Oh my God, did we have something scheduled today? I didn’t even...”

“No! No,” Sam laughed. “I just heard that you were sick and I thought I’d come... check on you? I guess? Is it okay if I come in?”

“Oh,” Joan sniffed and realized that her nose was visibly dripping. She tried to covertly clean it up with the same abused sleeve as she stepped back to let Sam in. “Sure, I... I’m sorry about the state of the place.”

“You always say that, and yet your apartment is always the cleanest- oh.”

Joan wanted to hide forever. Yeah, her apartment was normally perfect, she made sure of it. But this flu had taken her ability to think and her motivation to clean with it. It was mainly just the clutter on the table, and dishes... lots of dishes.

“Like I said,” she said, groaning at the state of her apartment. “Jesus, Sam, I’m sorry. I’m a mess today.” It was a testament to how bad she felt when she suddenly felt like crying. It probably would have happened a lot earlier had she not been so tired. Crying took a lot of effort.

“Joan, it’s fine!” Sam said with her usual lighthearted giggle. “Maybe you should sit down, though, you look a little bit like you’re going to...” she trailed off as she led Joan back to the couch.

Joan wasted no time covering up again, despite how pitiful she must look.

“I brought soup, if you’re hungry.”

She was just going to assume that it was past breakfast time, then. How long had she been laying there before Sam showed up?

“I...” she chuckled, sniffled. “I’m not, but I should probably eat. That’s so sweet of you...”

“Well, it’s just from the store,” Sam admitted as she took a container to the kitchen. It was followed by the sound of the microwave, as Sam never used the stove no matter where she was.

Joan listened, watched. She felt as if she were in the middle of some intense fog. If it weren’t for this flu, she could hold an actual conversation with her friend, ask her about her day, update her on research. Instead, she just sat there on the couch, rubbing her face and snuffling and coughing and wishing it would all go away.

And at some point, a bowl of soup was placed in her hands. She couldn’t smell a thing, but it looked good enough that her appetite didn’t leave her at the sight of food. Sam didn’t have any of her own, she must’ve already had lunch at work.

She sat next to Joan, hesitant at first as if wondering if she should be giving her space. Or perhaps she was just afraid of germs. Oh God, Joan must look so contagious right now. She should probably care about that more.

Joan ate quietly, thinking about what she should be talking about all the while. It was hard to focus on anything other than eating the soup and telling her stomach to keep it down.

“Did Owen tell you? That- that I was sick, I mean.”

“Hm? Oh, um, yeah. He did. He seemed kind of worried, to be honest.” Owen, of course. He’d seen her at her worst before, and she hated that thought. “But I figured it was just that flu that was going around.”

Joan nodded. “I believe so.” The AM had been an interesting place the past couple of weeks. Atypicals with high fevers presented a whole new set of problems. She winced at the thought of the near-delusional pyrokinetic they had to lead back to bed, after incinerating the door to their room.

“Joan?”

“Hm?” She’d stopped working on the soup halfway through. The bowl was carefully pulled from her grasp.

“Do you need tissues?”

Joan furrowed her brows as she tried to figure out what- Oh. Oh God. She brought her sleeve to her nose once again, and the fabric came back darkened with wetness. “God...”

A chuckle. “I mean do you have any around or should I go pick some up?”

“In my room. I just... haven’t bothered to get them.” She should have. Maybe her nose wouldn’t have been so sore if she used the actual tissues instead of her poor shirt.

Sam smiled softly and retrieved them. Joan wasted no time making use of one.

And then things were quiet again.

“I worked with Madeline today,” Sam said, breaking the silence. “She’s really warming up to everyone there. It’s almost going to be sad to see her go when the program’s done, you know? All of her friends are at the AM.”

Madeline, their newest telekinetic. She’d made so much progress as of late, and it seemed to help to have other Atypicals around to socialize with.

“I’m sure they’ll keep in contact. Relationships like that don’t go away easily.”

Sam hummed fondly.

Joan wasn’t sure when it happened, but she must have fallen asleep at some point. The only reason she came to that assumption was because she was rudely awakened by her own body. She pulled away and coughed, and coughed again. The change in position must have irritation something in her throat. There was a hand on her back, hesitantly comforting her as she struggled for control over her lungs.

How long had she been asleep on Sam’s shoulder? Poor Sam, how long had Joan forced her not to move? She didn’t get to ask, because the coughing caused a whole new set of problems.

Joan gasped and pressed a hand over her mouth.

“Joan? Joan are you-?” that was all she heard as she rushed toward the bathroom.

-----

A quiet, hesitant knock.

Joan rushed to wipe her mouth and flush the toilet before, “I’m alright,” she certainly didn’t sound alright. Her voice was hard for her to listen to.

“Are you sure?”

“I-” yes? Yeah. “I’m just sorry about your soup.”

Sam laughed, and the sound lifted Joan’s spirits just a bit. “Do you... I don’t know, want company or something?”

Joan sniffled and wiped her nose, it was running even more after she’d just spent the last ten minutes vomiting. “I’ll be out in a... minute. I think.”

“Sure,” Sam said softly.

Joan emerged after an attempt to look somewhat alive again. She combed her hair, which did very little when it was still greasy and gross. She was even more tired now than she had been before, if that was even possible.

Sam stood up from the couch when Joan appeared in the hallway. She always had a habit of doing that when someone entered the room.

Joan dragged a hand down her face with a sigh as she made her way over and flopped on the couch. Sam sat down.

“Okay, no offense but... you don’t look great.”

Joan chuckled. Understatement of the century. She’d just seen herself in the mirror, after all.

“Are you like, going to be okay?”

“Sam, it’s just the flu.” She tried to sound reasonable, which didn’t do a whole lot when she must have looked like she was on death's door.

“Right, you’re right.” Sam nodded.

“Really,” Joan said, offering a small to quell her friend’s worry. “I’ll be fine. You probably shouldn’t stay much longer, I don’t want you to... catch this.”

Sam scoffed. “I’ve already been exposed!”

Joan groaned. “Ugh, you’re right.”

“I should go, though. It’s almost Darwin’s dinner time.”

“Tell him hi for me?”

Sam giggled. “I will. Take care of yourself, okay? There’s more soup in the fridge if you ever feel like eating again. Feel better, Joan.”

“I’ll try.”

She still felt awful, but her mood was certainly lifted. Sam had that effect.
