Second Guesses

Ted second guessed himself a few times.

Maybe it was too personal, maybe it was too soon.

It wasn’t, he decided. And even if it was, it was too late to back out now.

Trent Crimm, The Independent, was his date for the banquet.

He adjusted his tie in the mirror for the tenth time. It looked fine, and he kind of wished it didn’t so he’d have something to do with his hands. He was never nervous for these kinds of things, he’d been to so darn many of them that it was hard to be nervous. Rich people threw parties for charity all the time, that was one of the first things he learned when he got a job as a big time coach.

He wasn’t even gonna go if he couldn’t find a date. Beard was his fallback, but he was usually busy on Saturday nights these days.

He wasn’t sure what possessed him that Trent was the first person he called.

But here he was.

Trent offered to pick him up and Ted couldn’t say no. Mainly because he couldn’t drive right in this darn country, and he’d never gotten the hang of cabs. And he enjoyed any extra time he could spend with Trent.

His door buzzed and Ted about had a heart attack right then and there. He looked good, right? He hoped he looked good, because he was fresh out of tie-fixing time.

He opened the door to find his date, the one and only Trent Crimm, looking like- like... someone who was incredibly handsome. Jeez he... wow. His hair was down, and his suit fit so perfectly that it made Ted want to wrap his arms around the man and never let go.

“Wow,” Trent raised his eyebrows. “You look fantastic.”

“Oh, well,” Ted felt himself blushing. “So do you.” That wasn’t even the half of it.

“Now, I must warn you,” Trent said as the got into his car. “I think that I might be coming down with something, so best not to... I dunno, catch it, I suppose.”

Ted frowned. “If you’re not feelin’ good-”

“Oh no no,” Trent waved it off. “It’s just a sore throat, nothing more.” Now that Ted was paying attention to something besides his fitted suit, he did notice a bit of a difference in Trent’s voice. It was thicker, sort of like how someone sounded after they’d been crying, or - of course - when they were starting to get sick. “I just thought that I should warn you before you try to eat off of my plate,” he chuckled.

“Ah,” a nod. “Yeah, that’s fair. I probably would’a done that.”

The place is beautiful, and like every other banquet Ted had been to. Warmly lit with reds and maroons and fancy dishware that had been bought in bulk and used for about a thousand other events.

There were a good few glances in their direction as the usher led them to their table.

“Being with me doesn’t violate some kind of journalist code, does it?” Ted whispered as they sat down.

“It’d be hard to care if it did.” And that was all Trent had to say about it, apparently. The reporter muffled a cough into his fist, shook his head, and read over the night’s agenda. “Lots of music,” he mused. “Are they good?”

“I guess we’ll find out!”

Trent started getting sniffly during the introduction to the children’s choir. He was clearly trying to keep it down, but Ted knew there was no stopping a runny nose once it was off. The poor man patted each of his pockets as he sniffled, desperately looking for a kerchief, a tissue, napkin, something. No luck.

Ted laid a hand on his back and discretely pressed a handkerchief into his hand. Trent nodded his thanks and pressed it to his nose.

Trent sneezed during one of the songs, and that was when he excused himself. Jeez, the poor guy. There was no worse place to have a cold than somewhere you were supposed to be quiet.

He returned with a pinker nose and a regular sniffle.

“Just say the word and we can get outta here,” Ted whispered as they received dessert. “You don’t sound great.”

“Ugh...” Trent sighed. “I’m sorry, Ted. This must be awful to listen to.”

“It is, but only because I know you’re not feeling good.”

Trent frowned. “It’s not terrible, I can make it through.”

“If you’re sure...”

Another man at their table was very chatty. It actually delighted Ted, he didn’t meet a lot of people who liked friendly conversation as much a he did. Ted had a good time talking to the guy about the differences between American and English cuisine. The man was big, had clearly enjoyed a good meal or two in his time. He gave a jolly chuckle as Ted made a joke about cookies versus biscuits.

Trent coughed, covering his mouth with the borrowed kerchief and turning away from the table as quickly as he could.

Ted rubbed his back as he turned back around. His eyes were watery from the force of it, and now his nose was clearly dripping. He pressed the kerchief to is face for a split-second, just long enough to soak up the stuff leaking out, and sighed out through his mouth.

“Pardon,” he said with a quick sniff.

Ted’s hand hadn’t left his back. “You’re feeling kinda warm. You sure this is just a cold? ‘cause it’s starting to look like the flu or somethin’.”

Trent didn’t answer right away, and that didn’t exactly fill him with confidence. “I... fuck.” he sighed. “You might be right.”

“Oh... huh.” Ted didn’t expect to get through to him that fast.

But onto the concerning part, Trent had no business sitting in a crowded room with loud music when he should be in bed.

“Hey, why don’t we get outta dodge, huh?”

“No, I can-”

“Nah, I’m not sure you can. Whaddya say?”

Trent didn’t argue any more.

They tried to sneak out the best they could. Ted left a good wad of cash in the donation box on their way to the coat room, and they were off.

“I’d offer to drive,” Ted said after his friend coughed miserably.

“But I’d really like to live,” Trent finished with that goofy smile that he always hid for some reason. Ted couldn’t help smiling even wider whenever he saw it. He was so used to Trent with that knowing look on his face that made most people uncomfortable.

Trent insisted on walking him to his door. Ted tried to refuse, and then it became clear to him that Trent didn’t want this night to end, even though it had to.

“I’m sorry, Ted,” he said again. “I think Emily must have been a carrier for whatever bug is going around the school.”

“Hey, quit that. It ain’t your fault! We’ll just have to do dinner even better next time!”

There was the adorable smile again. “Absolutely.”

“Go take care of yourself, huh?”

Trent nodded. He sniffed, looking exhausted. Ted knew from experience that the poor man would probably end up bedridden for a few days at least. He’d have to text him, ask him how he’s feeling, tell him how much he enjoyed tonight despite everything.

“Have a good night, Coach Lasso.”
