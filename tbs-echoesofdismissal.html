Echoes of Dismissal

Crying, gasping, pleading.

It’s not the first time Joan’s woken to that sound, unfortunately.

She’s out of bed and tossing her blankets aside in less than a second, rushing down the hallway to the living room.

Mark is awake, but clearly still engulfed by his dream. His face is wet with tears and he’s hardly getting a breath in as he hyperventilates. His nails are dug into the arm of the couch so deep it must hurt.

It doesn’t get easier when it’s family.

Joan’s dealt with plenty of others’ panic attacks and episodes. That was her job, after all. But it’s much harder to swallow the lump in her throat when it’s her own little brother sobbing and panicking and muttering to himself, “no no no no please no”.

She gathers herself and goes to his side, sitting next to him where his legs would be if he wasn’t curled in on himself, carefully setting a hand on his arm to get his attention. Teary eyes look at her, wide, as if he’s just realized she’s there.

“Mark,” she says, willing her voice not to shake. “Mark, can you hear me?”

He nods, but he has yet to stop to take a breath. “I- I can’t-” he sobs. “Joanie, I can’t-”

She rubs his arm and shushes him gently. “Take a breath, okay? With me?”

Joan wonders if he can really hear her over the horrors tumbling around in his head, and then he gives another minute nod.

“Take a breath, Mark,” she repeats.

He tries. It doesn’t work. He starts crying again, fear settling in as if he’s worried he’ll never be able to breathe again.

“It’s okay, it’s okay, again?”

They try again, but he can’t stop hyperventilating, and the tears are soaking his face. “I c- I can-” He wheezes. He looks at her soberly for a moment, maybe he’s coming own, before his expression crumbles again and he’s crying harder. “Oh God,” Mark whimpers. “I’m sorry, I’m sorry...”

It takes everything in her not to break down in tears herself. “What? Mark, it’s okay. You’re okay, you’re safe. I’m here.”

“I’m sorry,” he repeats, more quietly this time. But he’s starting to breathe again, and that’s a good step.

“I’m here.” She takes his hand, and he squeezes back.

Mark eventually wears himself out. Joan sleeps on the floor.

-----

There are better ways to wake up than on a hardwood floor with a single blanket underneath you. Joan doesn’t even have to sit up to know that everything’s going to hurt the moment she tries to move. She’s reached the point where she’s finally too old to sleep just anywhere without consequences.

But there are worse ways to wake up than to the smell of something delicious cooking. Wait...

She turns to find the couch abandoned, Mark’s bedding folded and set aside as usual.

As expected, her whole body aches as she stands. Joan takes the pillow and blanket she’d dragged out there and simply throws it onto Mark’s. She ventures into the kitchen to find it a mess and her brother contently cooking something on the stove.

“Morning!” He greets.

“Good morning,” she chuckles, folding her arms and leaning against the counter. “What’s going on here?”

“Oh, this?” he gestures grandly to the stove. “French toast. As a uh... thank-you-slash-apology for,” he pauses to clear his throat, averting his eyes. “For last night. That was... that was bad. I’m sorry.”

I’m sorry. Was that what he kept apologizing for last night?

Joan frowns. “Mark...”

“No, Joanie, I know what you’re going to say, and yes, I do need to apologize. I mean, you slept on the floor for Christ’s sake. How was that?”

“The floor was terrible,” she confirms. “But you shouldn’t feel the need to make it up to me every time something like that happens. That’s not how things work, alright?”

Mark’s attention is very much on the stove. He swallows hard, like he’s holding something back, like he wants to say something he knows she’s not going to like.

“How are you feeling after last night?”

“Good,” he answers a little too quickly, a little too cheerily.

“You’re sure?”

“Can we just-” he sighs. “Can we not talk about it anymore, please?” Joan’s hurt must be apparent on her face, because he continues. “Sorry, I mean, I brought it up, right? My fault.” Mark chuckles and goes back to cooking. “You like yours with cinnamon, right?”

Joan does him the favor of dropping it, even though all she wants to do is ‘therapize’ him and figure out why he’s letting his guilt rule him. But, she likes french toast, and spending time with her brother, and maybe this is something they’ll just have to come back to later.

“Yes, please!” She says.

-----

They eat, and talk, and laugh, all while avoiding the subject of the night before. Joan tries to get the image of Mark crying hysterically out of her mind.

All she can do is be there for him in any way he wants her to be. So she does.

He’s not well. He’s different today, as he often is after bad nights. He stare’s off into space in the middle of chewing, and then swallows before he’s done and ends up choking.

All the while, Joan is trying to ignore the headache creeping up behind her eyes and temples. It shouldn’t be much of a surprise considering where she slept, and how little she did. Her body’s had to adjust in some changes in routine since she got Mark back.

“Work today?” Mark asks between bites.

“No patients today,” she says. “I’d just be doing research and replying to e-mails, so I might as well stay in.”

Mark frowns. “You don’t have to stay home because of me, Joanie.”

“I know that I don’t,” is what she says, though she doesn’t exactly believe it. “But Sarah’s good at holding down the fort when I don’t need to be there.” She grunts and brings a hand to her head. “Not to mention, everything hurts,” she gives a lighthearted laugh in hopes Mark will understand that she’s not trying to poke at him.

He chuckles. “I hope french toast helps, then.”

“I think it does,” she says with an affirming nod.

-----

He mentions watching TV, gets settled on the couch. Joan goes to her room to change and gather her work for the day. When she comes back, the TV isn’t even on. Mark is staring at some spot on the wall with the distant look she knows to recognize in patients.

“Mark,” she says. He snaps out of it.

“Hey. Hi.”

She can’t resist asking this time. “Are you sure that you’re alright?”

“I’m fine, Joanie. Really.”

She gets settled next to him on the couch with her laptop and favorite soft throw. She looks up from the screen occasionally to judge her brother for the shows in his watch list.

He watches an episode of some show neither of them have heard of. She doesn’t pay attention, and Mark doesn’t mind.

The headache doesn’t go away.

Joan tries to massage her temple, her forehead. She really thought that the pain would fade away after a while, not get worse, but her limbs ache more than they had when she woke up on the floor and she now realizes that the headache has caused her to stop her work altogether.

And then, there’s the little problem that is her stomach.

She doesn’t want to blame Mark. Mark’s an amazing cook. But breakfast is digesting at a snail’s pace and Joan isn’t sure that bodes well.

She ignores it.

“Oh my God, she’s obviously the killer, you idiots,” Mark says to the TV, and he’s right, she’s definitely the killer. “They’re supposed to be these great detectives and yet they can’t see any of her obvious serial killer vibes?”

She doesn’t realize that she’s the one staring off this time until Mark,

“Joanie?”

She looks up to find Mark staring at her with concern. Concern for her, as if he hadn’t spent half the night before crying and panicking and being miserable.

“You okay?”

“I’m fine,” she says, and then realizes its an echo of the annoying dismissals Mark’s been giving her for weeks.

The last thing that she’s going to do is pretend like a simple headache matters when he’s dealing with everything.

-----

Mark brings up dinner before they’ve even had lunch.

“Maybe I can cook stir-fry?” he suggests out of nowhere.

Joan raises an eyebrow at him. “Sure?” She’s not hungry. In fact, she’s not feeling well at all. Breakfast is still sitting uneasily in her stomach like an indigestible rock, and Mark doesn’t seem to be affected.

He laughs. “What? I can cook twice in one day, can’t I?”

Joan rolls her eyes. “Of course, I just might have to do some grocery shopping before tonight.” Going out will give her an excuse to... well, not be her best. Normally the last thing she wants to do when she feels awful is go to the store, but today she just wants the distraction.

“We can go together,” Mark says as if they’re planning some exciting road trip. “I’ll grab some stuff for later this week, too.”

Or they could go together.

“Are you sure?”

“Yeah!” Mark clears his throat, hesitates. “Honestly, I don’t... I don’t really want to be alone right now, okay?”

“O-okay.”

They go together.

-----

Her skin aches, and she shivers from a gust of wind. She zips up her jacket on their way through the parking lot, while Mark’s wearing short sleeves with no concern in the world. That’s the first sign that she considers maybe she’s not just suffering from exhaustion or sleeping on hardwood in her thirties, but that maybe, maybe she’s getting sick.

Fantastic.

Mark’s going on about all of the things that he could cook this week, and all of the things he could do to their stir-fry tonight. It’s clear that he needs the distraction, food talk always helps, and he’s excited about it. Joan doesn’t have the heart to tell him that she can’t stand the thought of eating right now.

The sight of the meat counter alone causes the once subtle nausea to crank up to a ten. She turns away, finding another display particularly interesting as Mark thanks the butcher.

She’s sure now, that there’s no possible way she’s going to be able to eat whatever he cooks. It’s all downhill from here if she really is getting sick.

It’s a welcome distraction from her body’s failings when her phone vibrates. Mark nods and wanders off to let her answer in relative privacy.

“Hello?”

“Hi, Joan!” Sam, voice so kind that Joan can’t help but smile.

“Hey, what’s up? I’m at the store with Mark, so I can’t talk very long, but...”

“Oh, I was just wondering if you had the Atypical biology file?”

“Oh, um...” Joan’s brain isn’t exactly working on all cylinders. She takes a couple of seconds before, “Which one?”

“The one from the sixties?”

“I think that I do,” she says. She thinks she remembers seeing it, but damn it, why can’t she remember for certain? “Sorry, Sam, I...”

“Are you okay? You sound a little weird.”

Joan sighs, closing her eyes for a moment. It brings a bit of relief from the bright lights in the store that are doing her headache no favors. “Actually, I think that I’m coming down with something. My brain isn’t um, cooperating.”

Sam makes a sympathetic noise. “Jeez, I’m sorry.” And then, “What are you doing at the store, then? Shouldn’t you be, I don’t know, resting or something?”

“Mark wanted to pick up ingredients for dinner.”

“And you had to go with him...?”

Joan clears his throat, glances over to where Mark is in the process of chatting with an older lady who seems to be giving him cooking tips. “He... had a bad night, last night. I guess I just didn’t want to leave him alone. And honestly, I don’t think he wants to be alone.”

Sam’s quiet for a few seconds. Maybe that was too much to spring on her when she already worries about him as much as Joan does. “But he’s... okay now, right?”

“He’s fine,” Joan says quickly, an attempt to dissipate the anxiety creeping through Sam’s voice. “Just a little out of it, I think.”

“Well...” Sam pauses. “I hope both of you feel better, then.”

Joan can’t help but smile once again. “Thank you. Um, I’ll look for that file and text you, alright?”

“Sounds good!”

She pockets her phone and catches up to Mark, who seems to use her as an excuse to escape the conversation with the woman.

“Who was that?” Mark asks as he grabs a bottle of soy sauce and drops in the basket.

“Just Sam asking about a research file.”

“Ah,” he nods. And thankfully, that’s the end of the topic. Joan doesn’t feel like talking any more than she needs to.

-----

By the end of the shopping trip, Joan is far more exhausted than a trip to the store should ever make a person. The unsettled feeling in her stomach is more intense than ever, promising that something bad is going to happen soon.

Worst of all, though, is that Mark can tell. He’s quieted down a bit since he noticed that she’s also not talking as much. He’s staring off into space again. And it’s her fault.

-----

“I’m a little tired,” she says when they get home. “I might lie down for a while.”

Mark chuckles, “What, the floor wasn’t restful enough for you?”

She scoffs. “Surprisingly, no.”

He’s already started putting the groceries away with the excitement of someone who’s about to cook a masterpiece. “Goodnight, then! I’m gonna start chopping the veggies.”

“Have fun!” she calls as she head down the hallway.

She’s about to go into her bedroom when she stops. Aware of the shivery, sensitive feeling in her skin and seeping into her bones. Those... those are chills. Frowning nervously, Joan makes a detour into the bathroom and fishes a thermometer from a drawer that desperately needs to be reorganized. She turns it on and inserts it into her ear, avoiding her reflection in the bathroom mirror as she does so.

100.1 is hardly a fever, she tells herself. But it is a fever, which confirms that this is going to get worse.

Joan goes to bed and hopes that somehow, she’ll feel better when she wakes up.

-----

Joan wakes a few hours later to the smell of stir-fry and an overwhelming urge to vomit. She has a mere few seconds to get out of the tangled mess of blankets and across the hall to the bathroom, ignoring the sudden rush of cold air that has her shivering violently.

She closes the door behind her, and then makes it to the toilet just in time.

Fuck.

Mark’s music is echoing throughout the kitchen, and he’s singing terribly along with it. He doesn’t know, and better yet, he’s having a good day.

She sits there for... ten minutes? She isn’t sure. Before the nausea dies down and she’s no longer in immediate danger of vomiting. That’s probably as good as it’s going to be. Joan scrubs the slime from her face and flushes the toilet before pulling herself to her feet.

The sink supports her as she looks at her reflection with distaste. Her eyes and nose are both red and watery from throwing up. She shudders. The bathroom is freezing and all she’s wearing is a t-shirt, and...

And she should probably take her temperature again.

Joan does so, and regrets it instantly. Her fever rose to 101.5 just while she slept. Whatever this bug is, it’s not something little that’s going to resolve itself with more sleep.

The realization doesn’t stop her from making herself look less sick and going to join Mark in the kitchen, though.

“You’re awake!” Mark greets. “Dinner’s almost ready, I was afraid you were going to sleep through it!”

She swallows thickly at the smell of the components cooking together. She can’t eat, not when she’s sick, not when she just vomited less than an hour ago. “Mark-” But her voice is hoarse and he doesn’t hear her. Instead, she lets him continue his excited ramble.

“I was thinking about calling Sam and inviting her over if that’s okay with you?”

Joan blinks, nods. “I- yes, of course. She knows she’s welcome here any time.”

He smiles brightly. “Cool,” he pulls out his phone, and then says, “Oh! Here, taste this, tell me if it’s too sweet?” Mark hands her a spoonful of something brown and translucent and she can’t bring herself to refuse.

Instead, she nods, and stares at the liquid as Mark finds Sam’s contact in his phone.

“Hey! So I made like, a lot of food, and I thought maybe you’d want to join us for dinner?”

Joan watches as his smile just gets wider in the few seconds he’s listening to Sam’s voice.

“Yeah, you have time! Oh well, I mean if you wanted to bring dessert, I don’t think we’d say no,” he laughs. Joan tries not to wince.

She sips half of the spoon, and her tongue is met with something that she’s sure would taste fine on any other day. But yes, it’s too sweet. And too salty, and too sour, and too- oh God, she doesn’t feel well.

Suddenly, Mark’s smile fades. Joan wonders if she poorly schooled her features, and then realizes that it’s something Sam said that has him looking like that. “What do you mean?”

Joan frowns.

“Uh... fine, I guess. I don’t-” Mark looks at her with an intense expression that promises a conversation is to be had. Uh oh. “She did? No, I didn’t... Sam, it’s okay.” He chuckles, the kind he uses to lighten the mood, especially with Sam. “Yeah, we’ll see you in a bit.”

She’s still trying to ignore the taste of the stir fry sauce on her tongue, but nausea might be the least of her problems now. Mark set his phone down without breaking eye contact.

“Sam wanted to know how you were feeling?”

Joan clears her throat. “She did?”

“Yeah, apparently you told her that you were getting sick and she was worried.”

Joan has every apology or defense on her tongue. But it’s no match for the sickly-sweet sauce that’s also sitting there. She finds herself darting to the bathroom with a hand over her mouth.

-----

Her throat hurts. Her stomach hurts, her head hurts. Where awful nausea sat in her gut is now just a tired, worn out feeling as if her stomach muscles have had enough. It hasn’t stopped her from dry heaving.

She can hear that Mark’s still cooking, though more slowly now. Perhaps lying to him did more damage than being honest would have. She can’t help but find the realization humorous considering how many times she’s explained that to patients.

She sits against the wall, knees pulled to her chest, feeling particularly miserable. Not even because of the illness, but because she tried to make something better and only succeeded in making it worse. Of course.

Footsteps get louder as they approach the door, and Joan hopes that he’s just passing by to get to the linen closet.

A knock. “Joanie?” The gentle, careful tone she remembers Mark using with her when she got in a fight with their parents or had a bad breakup.

“Yeah?”

“Are you done puking?”

“I... think so, yes.” She clears her throat. “For now.”

“Do you need anything?”

Joan sniffs. “No, I’m okay.”

“Ah.” He leaves, footsteps getting quieter as he goes back to the kitchen.

Pushing people away is another thing that Joan tells patient’s not to do. Funny.

But then there’s another knock. He came back. “Can I come in?”

Joan sighs. “I- sure?” she says, realizing that it doesn’t exactly matter now, does it?

He enters with a cup of water and sets it next to her.

Mark immediately plops on the floor, sitting cross-legged next to her as if the bathroom floor is exactly where he belongs right now. She raises an eyebrow at him, but she's far too tired to question it. It's Mark, of course he's going to do something a little weird.

"So," he says, as casually as ever. "Why didn't you say anything?"

Joan's... exhausted. She still feels sick to her stomach and her head hurts and she's not even sure she can form coherent sentences right now. Yet, she somehow manages a, "I don't know..."

"Does that kind of answer fly when you're working with a patient?"

Joan sighs, and finds herself tearing up before she can stop it. Her lip quivers and it takes everything in her being not to break down right now.

"Joanie...?" Mark's voice loses its sarcasm when he notices.

This is exactly what she was trying to avoid. Mark doesn't need any more stress and anxiety right now.

Joan hugs her knees and sniffs, biting her lip to hold back a sob and feeling so... young.

She steels herself and wipes her eyes on the sleeve of her shirt. “I... suppose I didn’t want to make a bad day worse.”

Mark frowns, eyes widening. “Jesus... that’s- that’s some pretty self-sacrificial bullshit, don’t you think?”

Yes, she supposes it is.

“How long-?”

“Just since this afternoon,” she says with another sniffle. “Mark, I wasn’t trying to-” to what? Lie to him? She sighs. “I should have told you, I’m sorry.”

“Well,” Mark shrugs and lets his back bump against the wall. “I guess Sam and I are going to be eating a lot tonight. I mean, I’m going to assume that you don’t want stir fry right now.”

Joan can’t help a groan at the mere thought of eating. “... sorry.”

“Make it up to me by not hiding these kinds of things just to spare my fragile feelings, Joanie.” He laughs. “I mean, give me some credit.”

“Right,” she agrees. There are some things she’ll never tell Mark about, like the fact that her practice is slowly fading or that she’s constantly afraid of the AM coming after her for one reason or another. Or that she never wants to let him out of her sight ever again lest he somehow get kidnapped and slip into a coma once again.

There’s a knock on the front door and Mark stands up. “Welp, there’s Sam.”

“I’m okay,” she says before he can ask. “I’ll be out in a few minutes.”
