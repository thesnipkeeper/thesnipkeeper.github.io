Title: Voicemail
Fandom: Bright Sessions
Summary: Joan learns that - for the first time in years - she has people that she can rely on when she needs help
Word count: 3.4k

Voicemail

It starts with a stomach ache after breakfast. She can’t imagine what could have gone wrong with toast of all things.

Joan tries to ignore it as she walks into work and begins her day like normal. She’ll drink some tea, maybe have a lighter lunch. No problem.

Except, the feeling doesn’t go away.

Unrelenting cramps nagging at her, yet nothing relieves them. And her head is starting to hurt too, for some reason? She can hardly stand upright without feeling horribly sick.

Sam runs into her in the break room while she’s making tea, humming something that would be adorable any other day but today.

“Morning!” She greets cheerily.

“Morning,” Joan says, and she knows she sounds... un-enthused. But is that anything out of the ordinary?

Apparently today it is. “You okay?”

“Fine,” she grits her teeth to endure another horrible cramp in her stomach. The idea of drink the tea in front of her now is nauseating. Sam, of course, doesn’t believe it for a second. “Um... just a stomach ache,” she admits.

Sam makes a sympathetic sound. “Jeez, I’m sorry. Do you need anything?”

Right now, all she wants to do is go back to her office, with her nice warm, unappetizing tea, and wrap herself in a sweater to combat the chills slowly making their way through her.

She shakes her head. “No, I’m okay. Thank you.”

-----

She can hear Owen’s footsteps before he appears. And Joan is already quite sure that she doesn’t feel like dealing with him right now, not while she’s fighting nausea and shuddering, and in the middle of seeing exactly how sick she really is.

“Joan,” he says, taking a couple steps back and standing in the doorway. “You’re taking your temperature?”

Joan looks up with wide eyes, and pulls the thermometer from her ear as it beeps. “I am,” she confirms, already annoyed with him.

He waits a good few seconds for more of an explanation, while she simply puts the device away with a steeled expression. “Are... you feeling alright?”

Joan swallows, clears her throat, and sighs before looking back up to him. He frowns with sad eyes, a look of realization that tells her he’s reading her.

“As a matter of fact,” she swallows again. “I think that I’m coming down with some kind of bug.”

“And your temp...?”

She’s considering telling him to go away, that it’s none of his business, or to simply lie. Instead, she says, “Not... great.” She subtly lays a hand on her belly, as if that’s going to do anything to quell the nausea.

Owen frowns. “Joan, I can take care of things here if you need to go home.”

“I’ll be fine,” and then another “I will!” at the disbelief on his face. “I can work.”

“I don’t think...”

“This isn’t your decision, Owen,” Joan bites. Bitterness is on the edge of her voice, reminding him that he has no place in her life these days. He’s not in charge of her, it’s none of his business. (And she really wants him to go away before something bad happens.)

That doesn’t stop him from freaking out when she suddenly lunges for her trash bin and starts throwing up.

“Oh my God, Joan-” He gasps, rushing in. Except, now he can’t exactly do anything but stand uselessly while she heaves.

She holds up a hand, telling him to stay away, and he does. She ends up holding the bin to her chest, breathing heavily, eyes watering. Another bout isn’t far behind.

"I-"

Joan swallows hard at the building nausea, but doesn't move to look at him.

"P-please go."

He’s reluctant, probably tempted to do something to help as if that’s the only thing he knows how to do. But he nods and steps out. Thank God.

-----

Joan sniffs, scrubs at her eyes. She knows that the last thing she should be worrying about is embarrassing herself in front of Owen. As if she’s worried what he thinks, right? But it doesn’t stop her from crying pathetically in her office.

Once she’s not in immediate danger of throwing up, she goes to work fixing herself, cleaning up, trying not to cry any more.

She has to pause when a wave of nausea returns. A shaky hand (when did her hands start shaking? God damn it...) goes to her mouth as she swallows carefully, eyes closed. Breathe in, breathe out, no throwing up right now after she literally just finished cleaning.

The nausea does fade after a good couple of minutes standing as still as a statue.

Joan shudders, wraps her sweater tighter around herself, and grabs her purse.

She makes her way to Owen’s office, or just outside the door of Owen’s office, before she stops. She... she doesn’t want to face him again. Ever. Of course Owen isn’t going to think badly of her, but he... he’s her ex.

Still, she knocks on his door.

“Come in!” he looks up from his work and his eyes widen with that familiar concern that shouldn’t really be directed toward her. “Joan, how- how are you feeling?”

Joan sighed. “I think that I’m going to go home, if that’s still alright with you.”

“Of course,” Owen quickly nods. “Please, take care of yourself.”

She clears her throat. “Thank you, Owen.” Her stomach begins to gurgle ominously. She should get out of there.

“Joan-” but Owen’s voice stops her.

“Mhm?”

“Are you alright?”

“Clearly not,” she tries to pretend like her voice didn’t break. Apparently she wants to cry again.

He chuckles a bit and nods. “Right, yes, I just meant... do you need anything?”

She thinks for a moment. “No, I’ll be okay.”

“You’ll let Sam or I know if you do, though? Please?”

Joan swallows thickly and nods. “Yes, of course,” she says, with absolutely no intention of following through. This isn’t the first time she’s dealt with a bug on her own. She’ll be fine without bothering anyone else, she always is.

-----

She barely manages to close her door behind her before she’s rushing to the bathroom to throw up once again, hand clamped over her mouth.

To her horror, a wave forces its way up her throat just before she’s over the toilet. She can’t begin to react to the mess on the tile floor before she’s throwing up again. Joan allows herself a whimper now that she’s alone.

Her stomach makes it clear that it's not done torturing her yet, with an angry growl that bubbles in her gut, promising to make her day as miserable as possible.

She just wishes she’d brought a blanket in with her. God, she’s freezing. The rational, scientist part of her brain reminds her that it’s her elevated temperature that’s causing her to feel cold, but it doesn’t stop her from fantasizing about turning the apartment’s heat up and wrapping herself in every blanket she owns.

Instead, she pulls a towel from the shelf and wraps it around her shoulders, still shuddering as she does.

Joan isn’t sure how long she sits there, or when exactly she ends up curled in the corner where the bathtub and the wall meet (far away from the puddle she still needs to clean up). Everything hurts, her skin is hot and oversensitive, the slightest bump against something causes her to whimper.

She’s at the point where she’s not sure how nauseated she is, or whether it’s safe to go back to bed. Or whether she can move without vomiting.

-----

Joan wakes up.

Her head hurts from sleeping against the wall. No, her whole body hurts.

How long has she been asleep...?

The room smells of stale vomit and Joan doesn’t even think before going to work cleaning.

She flushes the toilet, carefully averting her eyes from the contents. The mess on the floor isn’t as bad as she thought it was, and she manages to get it cleaned up enough for now.

The big challenge, Joan realizes, is getting back to bed.

She works up the will to grab the edge of the sink and pull herself up onto shaky legs. Her head swims for a moment, her stomach complains about the sudden movement. She stands still until both feelings pass, and then she grabs the waste bin and takes slow, careful steps out.

Joan supports herself on the wall and various pieces of furniture for the few feet it takes to get to her bed. She feels unsteady in every sense of the word, but she makes it.

-----

Joan wakes up.

She fell asleep again?

She shivers violently under the blankets she managed to pile on top of herself. Her vision swims and darkens when she turns to check the time. It’s only seven, she’s been home from work for less than five hours.

That’s when her body reminds her why she’s awake in the first place. An awful cramp in her belly intensifies, causing her to curl up and whine in pain, pressing a hand into the flesh as if that’s going to solve anything.

She has a split second to lean over the edge of the bed before she throws up. Most of it makes it into the bin. She’s beyond caring about a mess right now.

Joan vaguely thinks that she should be drinking water or some sort of electrolyte solution to try to keep herself hydrated. The idea of getting out of bed makes her feel sick all over again, and yet...

Water. If anything to rinse the horrible taste in her mouth. She can do this.

Joan shudders as she pulls the blankets back and her skin meets the cold air of her apartment. Maybe some rest did her body good, because she’s slightly less shaky, or perhaps its sheer determination that’s forced it to cooperate.

Her vision still starts to go black just before she gets to the kitchen, so Joan takes a quick seat at the dining table and closes her eyes for a moment.

When she opens them, she spots something on the counter. Her phone.

She knew that she was lying to Owen when she said she’d ask for help. This is not the first time she’s been horribly sick on her own, it’ll likely not be the last. She’s always been able to take care of herself, and she’d be lying if she said she doesn’t pride herself on it at least a little bit.

Joan stands when she’s finally able. She wastes no time quickly filling a glass with water and going back to bed, phone in-hand this time. Probably a good thing to have around.

She takes a few sips until her stomach loudly protests. She lies back, hoping to let the water settle.

It doesn’t.

Joan vomits it up a few minutes later. And maybe her fever’s going up, because she begins crying.

Perhaps it’s panic that she can’t keep water down, or simply misery, but she sobs hard. It does nothing good for her stomach, and she ends up heaving into the bin.

She falls asleep minutes later, unable to keep her eyes open any longer.

-----

She wakes up with a headache, a sore throat, and a dry mouth. She’s still shivering so violently.

She heaves over the edge and into the bin, as if it’s some sort of routine at this point. What comes up is foul-tasting bile as she’s certainly puked up everything else in her stomach. This would normally be when she drinks water, but she just... doesn’t want to throw up any more than she has to.

She should take her temperature. It’s no-doubt gotten much higher than it was at the AM, which is hardly a good thing when it was already over one hundred degrees.

It’s ten o’clock.

Joan starts to worry.

She glances at her phone on the nightstand, cogs in her fever-addled brain struggling to turn enough to think about her next step.

No... no, she’s not bothering anyone else. She’s not going to die of dehydration or fever in one day. She may feel terrible, but her life isn’t in danger. There’s no need to call for help. No one needs to deal with something she can handle on her own.

-----

Joan doesn’t even have time to process being awake before she’s leaned over the bin again, heaving uselessly and causing her stomach to ache with the effort. Her head hurts from the force of vomiting. Her whole body aches, feverish fatigue deep in her bones.

She tears up a bit, but doesn’t have the energy to truly cry.

She desperately wants to go back to sleep, but the aches and pains make it impossible.

Her fevered brain has her wondering if maybe she is going to dehydrate. If this were anyone else, she’d be taking them to the doctor right about now. It doesn’t take her long to realize that she’s properly scared, because she can barely turn over in bed let alone drive to the ER.

Joan’s hardly aware of herself grabbing her phone and clumsily finding a specific contact.

It rings. And rings

And rings.

A glance at the clock beforehand would have been useful because she now realizes that Sam is not one to be awake at midnight.

“You’ve reached Samantha Barnes! Please leave a message and I’ll try to get back to you” the beep that follows causes Joan to wince.

“Hi Sam,” it’s been so long since she’s used her voice and God, she sounds wrecked. She pauses to clear her throat and continues. “It’s- it’s Joan. Um.” She takes a shaky breath and realizes that she’s crying. Shit shit shit, this is... this is a mess. “I just- I know it’s late, which is why you’re asleep, obviously, but I- I’m not feeling well, and I’m- it’s making me a little nervous?” Another shaky breath. “This was a mistake, I’m sorry. Please ignore this message, I’m fine, honestly.” And she hangs up.

She sniffles, stuffs her face in her pillow, and tries to pretend that she hadn’t just embarrassed herself more than she already did at work.

Eventually, she falls into an uncomfortable, feverish sleep.

-----

The next time Joan wakes up, it’s not to a need to throw up or an unbearable stomach cramp, (granted, those two things are both lingering and probably will be for a good while) but she wakes to the sound of someone else in the apartment.

Her first reaction is panic. Her heart starts racing, wondering if her death will be brought not by a stomach flu but by a home intruder. But then she hears humming, the same song Sam had been humming the day before.

Joan relaxes. Glances at the clock.

It’s... noon. She tries to remember anything before she fell asleep last night, but comes up empty.

She pulls herself out from under the covers and finds that she’s no long shuddering violently at the impact of the outside world. While still tired and unsteady, she’s not nearly as lightheaded as she had been. Her stomach is still unsettled, but she supposes rest can’t fix everything. Oh no...

She glances down at the bin with horror and a new wave of humiliation, only to find that it’s been cleaned out and re-line with a new bag. Huh.

Joan pads out of her room, following the content hum of her friend until she finds Sam doing dishes.

“Sam?” Her voice is rough, but it’s loud enough to cause the poor thing to jump and yelp in surprise.

“Oh!” Sam says, hand still over her heart. “You’re awake! You didn’t have to get out of bed, I was about to come check on you.”

Joan certainly feels like she’s missing something. She takes a seat at the island where Sam’s laid dishes out to dry. “Um, what... what happened last night?”

Sam furrows her brows in confusion. “Well, you called me at like midnight, and-”

“Oh my God,” Joan groans, dipping her head in humiliation as the memory comes back to her. “I’m so sorry, I was barely conscious.”

“No no! Calm down, Joan it’s okay!” she offers a reassuring smile. “Um, I got the message a couple hours later when I woke up,” the nightmare part goes unsaid. “And I tried calling back, and you sounded... worse than your message did.”

Joan has absolutely no memory of a second phone call.

“I came over and you were burning up, and... this doesn’t ring any bells?”

She thinks really hard and finds that she has vague memories of cold hands on her forehead, Sam’s ‘freaking out’ voice, a cup of water at her lips. “Starting to,” she says.

Sam nods. “Anyway, after a while you were finally able to keep some ibuprofen down and get some sleep.”

“Ah,” Joan sighs. “That explains why I feel better, then.”

“Yeah,” Sam says with wince. “God, Joan, I almost dragged you to the ER, you were so out of it.”

Joan frowns. “I’m- I’m sorry. I didn’t mean to scare you.”

“I know you didn’t,” Sam’s voice goes soft. “I’m glad you called, I wouldn’t have wanted you to deal with that alone.”

Joan swallows. It’s really a punch in the gut after all of her rationalization for not calling.

“So um, how do you feel about some toast?”

She can’t help but cringe. “A couple of saltines, maybe?”

“Coming right up!”

Joan smiles and sleepily leans against the counter, palm supporting her head. “Can’t imagine this is how you wanted to spend your Saturday.”

Sam scoffs and sets a paper plate with a small stack of saltines in front of her. “I find it hilarious that you think I had Saturday plans.”

Joan chuckles. “Fair point.”

She nibbles for a couple of minutes, and sips on some water, and tries to ignore her stomach’s complaints.

Sam weaves around the island and lays the back of her hand against Joan’s temple, forehead, then her neck. Her hands are cold, but blissfully so, and Joan can’t help but enjoy the touch just a bit.

She hums thoughtfully. “I’m... not sure if you feel cooler because of the medicine, or...”

“The fever isn’t going away any time soon, trust me,” Joan says. “I... I run a bit warm when I’m sick.”

“Really? Jesus, that must suck.”

It does, Joan thinks- realizes. She’s never thought about it before, how miserable she is even when she catches a simple cold. Most times, it’s as if her body’s forcing her to lie down and rest.

They both look over at a knock on the door.

“Oh, that’s, um...” Sam clears her throat and doesn’t bother finishing her sentence before opening the door.

Joan tenses at the sight of Owen standing awkwardly in her doorway, grocery bags in-hand.

“Thank you so much, Owen,” Sam says, taking the bags from him.

He takes a single, hesitant step in and offers a kind smile. “It wasn’t a problem. Um...” his gaze finds Joan, and she feels particularly uncomfortable under his scrutiny when she’s in sweats and probably looks like an absolute mess. “How are you doing?”

“I’m...” that’s a good question. “I’m alright, I think. Thanks to Sam listening to a delirious voicemail in the middle of the night.” It’s meant to lighten the mood, but he looks mildly horrified at the thought.

“Well, if you need to take work off on Monday, let me know,” he says. “I can handle whatever you need me to!”

“Th- thank you. I’m sure I’ll be functional by Monday.” She’s not sure at all, but she already knows she’s going to end up at work anyway.

She moves her attention to where Sam’s been unpacking the bags. Sports drinks, popsicles, electrolyte solution... she imagines Sam sending him a worried text at some point. Joan’s eyes lock on a box of tea among all of the other things, the sort of tea Owen always picked up when she wasn’t feeling well.

He seems to follow her gaze, because he gives a sheepish smile.

Joan decides that she’s not going to cry again. “You two...” she says, and ends up getting the attention of both of them. “Thank you.” For being there when she needs them, for showing up in the middle of the night, for being her friends after she spent so many years without anyone at all.

“Joan, you don’t have to thank us,” Sam says quickly, which is a bold thing coming from someone who spent at least some amount of time cleaning up puke.

“Still...” She wants nothing more than to continue the heartfelt moment, but her stomach turns angrily and she lays a hand on it with a grimace. She once again rushes off to vomit.

But the bright side, she supposes, is that she isn’t scared this time. Just very, very sick.
