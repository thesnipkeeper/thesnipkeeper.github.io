Ranch Style Home

Patrick glances over at him as he yawns for the hundredth time. He chuckles and turns his eyes back to the road. "I did tell you to go to sleep early last night."

David shakes his head in an attempt to clear it. It does nothing. "I did! I mean, I thought I did. I don't know why I'm so tired." It was even harder than usual to get out of bed that morning. And it's remained, the bone-deep fatigue that makes his limbs feel like lead.

"Maybe we'll pass a Starbucks on our way," Patrick says slyly, knowing full well that David’s weak for an impromptu coffee trip.

He smiles at his husband. Patrick’s in a good mood, and it’s pretty fucking adorable how excited he is to see his family. He’s spent most of the car ride so far talking about his parents and his childhood, telling him all sorts of stories.

David was nervous about spending four days with Patrick’s parents at first. But now, seeing his husband all giddy and cute, he can’t help but share his excitement.

They do eventually find the coffee David so desperately needs, and soon he’s happily sipping on a caramel macchiato and using his caffeine high to tell Patrick everything he’d like to do this week. Patrick humors him.

It's not long after the coffee's gone that David realizes while he feels more talkative, he's still tired. His body is still determined to weigh him down and his heart is beating a little too fast. The caffeine starts to jumble his head just a little.

"I'm surprised you haven't said anything about stopping for food yet," Patrick chuckles.

Oh yeah, food. David's eyes widen. He isn't even sure he ate breakfast that morning. Patrick looks equally as worried to hear that news when he says it out loud. Nevertheless, they agree that if they keep driving they’ll be able to make it to the Brewers’ before lunchtime. He’s still not very hungry, the coffee having subsided his appetite for now.

David must have fallen asleep at some point because suddenly he’s being woken by a kiss on his cheek. As he comes to, he realizes that the hum of the engine is missing and they’re parked in the driveway of a cute blue house.

“Mmh’h?” he rubs his eyes, giving a jaw-cracking yawn. Patrick looks so excited he can’t begin to feel grumpy about having to wake up.

“We’re here,” Patrick says with a smile. “Let’s go inside. We can come back out for our stuff later.”

Before he knows it, his husband is already getting out of the car and urging David to do the same. He climbs out of the vehicle with shaky, post-nap legs and smiles sleepily as Patrick’s hand lands on the small of his back and they walk up to the door together.

Marcy immediately envelopes them both in a hug when she opens the door, closely followed by a handshake and nod from Clint who seems to understand David can only take so many affectionate, smothering hugs in one day.

He tries to keep his spirits up as the excited chatting continues. The last thing he wants Patrick’s parents to think is that he doesn’t want to be here. He’s just so tired.

Patrick catches his eyes for a moment and smiles before pausing his conversation with his mom. “I think I’m going to take David upstairs,” he says, wrapping an arm around David’s waist. “He’s a little tired, probably wants to nap.”

“Aw, I understand,” Marcy says.

David puts on an apologetic smile. “Didn’t get a lot of sleep last night? I’m so sorry. Thank you for letting us stay here.”

“Don’t worry about it, we won’t need you two until dinner.” She lays a hand on David’s arm before Patrick leads him upstairs.

David’s been here before, for one night. They spent Christmas day here last year after having Christmas Eve with David’s family. He still doesn’t feel too comfortable being in a house he isn’t familiar with, but he knows that he’s going to have to get used to it. Marcy and Clint are family now, after all, and he doesn’t want to be a stranger in their house.

“Are you okay?” Patrick asks quietly as they turn down the hallway. “You’re acting kind of weird.” It’s the same gentle, trying-to-be-casual tone Patrick uses when he thinks David’s on the verge of a panic attack. Maybe this whole thing does have him feeling a little anxious, but not to that extent.

“I’m fine!” David’s voice unintentionally goes up at the end as he shakes his head and turns his nose to the ceiling for a moment.He continues when the confusion doesn’t leave his husband’s beautiful face. “I’m just, really tired. I’m not- I don’t think it’s because of anything...” that last part is joined by both of his hands waving around his head. And it’s true, he feels... well, okay, not fine, but what he does feel he can’t exactly pinpoint right now. The words “I don’t know” would just cause Patrick to start asking questions when what he really wants right now is to sleep.

The corner of Patrick’s mouth quirks up. “Okay,” he says softly. “Please tell me if something else is up, though.”

David nods. It’s a promise he made early in their relationship, to tell Patrick when he’s feeling bad instead of keeping it hidden until it sends him spiraling. “I promise, but honestly right now all I want is a nap.” Lunch is forgotten, apparently. He’s not even hungry, which is a terrifying thought.

“Good plan. Actually, I might join you in a bit.”

“Mmm, I can not promise that I can stay awake for much longer.”

A chuckle. “That’s okay, David. Get some sleep.”

David immediately finds his way under the covers of Patrick’s old bed. He wastes no time getting comfortable while Patrick adjusts some things in the room.

“Now I’ll just hope your parents won’t hate me for showing up to their house and immediately going to sleep.”

“My parents don’t hate you, David. I think they understand that you’re not a morning person.”

David huffs out a laugh, letting his eyes fall closed.

He only feels a little guilty as he half-listens to Patrick quietly bring their bags into the room. It sounds like he had to make a couple trips, David thinks he should thank him. Before he manages to open his eyes again, though, he feels a weight on the other side of the bed, and soon an arm wrap around his waist. Patrick nuzzles his neck, sighing contently before they both fall asleep.

David shifts, vaguely aware of a dull cramp in his stomach where the full feeling from the coffee never really went away. He hears quiet conversation downstairs, but he’s not quite ready to be awake. Patrick’s presence is sadly missing. He tries to fall back asleep despite the voices from the kitchen, and he succeeds for a few blissful minutes.

The cramp sharpens, surfaces. David’s eyes shoot open, he scrambles to get up. Suddenly, he know something very bad is going to happen if he doesn’t get out of bed right now. But it’s far too late for that.

He hears the splatter on the floor of Patrick’s childhood bedroom before he’s aware that he’s just vomited on the floor. David doesn’t have time to freak out about it before it happens again.

And then he’s left panting for breath, tears in his eyes from the exertion that are quickly building when he sees what’s just happened. David stares at the puddle, heart racing, trying so hard not to cry because it’s just going to make things worse.

He needs to- he needs to clean up. Right? But how does he do that when he doesn’t know where anything is, and oh God, what are Patrick’s parents going to think when they hear that he just puked in their house?

David snuffles desperately. He’s reminded what Patrick told him earlier that day. Tell him if something is up. This probably isn’t the ‘something’ Patrick had in mind, but David feels himself nearing an anxiety attack that’s only going to worsen the roiling in his stomach and he feels that warrants an emergency text.

David: i need help

David: something happened

He hears the conversation downstairs taper off, Patrick’s voice telling his parents that he’s going up for a minute. The thump of footsteps on the staircase before the door to the bedroom finally opens.

“Hey, what’s-? Oh, David...” Patrick’s eyes find him immediately, and then the mess in front of him.

“I-” David takes a deep breath. This is not the time to stop breathing. He folds his arms over his stomach miserably. He can’t look Patrick in the eyes, he just can’t. This is too humiliating, and he’s probably not done. God, what if he’s sick and just brought a virus into the Brewer’s house? “I didn’t know what to do. If you can like, just show me where-”

A kiss softer than it has the right to be is pressed to his temple before Patrick disappears for a moment. He hears various noises from the bathroom down the hall before his husband reappears with a roll of paper towels, grocery bag, and disinfectant spray. David sits on the bed uselessly.

“I’m sorry,” he finally blurts out when Patrick starts soaking up the gross liquid with paper towels. He supposes it’s a good thing that all he’s had is coffee today.

“It happens,” Patrick says, far too dismissive for someone who’s in the middle of cleaning up someone else’s vomit. He looks up. “Do you feel like you’re going to throw up again?”

David shakes his head uncertainly, even though he’s absolutely certain that the remaining nausea isn’t going away on its own. “I don’t- I don’t know... oh... fuck...” His arms tighten around his stomach, he leans over, pressing them into it as if it would help at all. He stands up, steadying himself on the bed frame.

“Bathroom...?” Patrick says cautiously.

David clamps a hand over his mouth and nods before rushing out of the room.

“I haven’t said anything to my parents, they’re still downstairs,” Patrick assures, hand running up and down David’s back in gentle strokes.

David’s been curled up in bed since the whole debacle. There’s a particular taste left in his mouth, and he decides he’s never going to be able to drink coffee again.

“David, will you look at me?”

“No.”

“David...”

David sighs and looks up at his husband. “That was awful,” he says. “this is awful.”

“I know,” Patrick says with a sympathetic frown. “But it’s okay! Really, it is.”

“It’s not okay. We were supposed to have fun this week. What if I have some sort of... medieval plague? My death is really going to put a damper on things, Patrick.”

“Maybe, but I’ll throw a beautiful funeral for you.”

“Well,” a sigh, an eye roll. “Thank you. That’s very nice.”

Patrick gives a long look, biting his lip like he wants to say something. “If it’s okay with you, I think I should probably mention this to-”

“No. No way.”

“Okay, and I understand! But mom’s about to make dinner and I’m going to take a wild guess and say you don’t feel like eating four-cheese lasagna at the moment?”

His stomach sours. “Oh God no.”

“Can I just let her know that you’re not feeling very good?”

David huffs. “If it’s necessary. I just- I feel bad that I show up to their house and immediately ruin everything.”

He recoils in offense when Patrick laughs. Laughs!

“David, I know that your childhood wasn’t exactly... traditional. Your parents, as wonderful as they are, weren’t particularly nurturing, right?”

David raises an eyebrow. He really hopes Patrick is going somewhere with this, because he’d prefer to return to wallowing in self-pity. He sighs tiredly. “Get to the point, please.”

“They care about you, David. To them, you’re a part of the family.”

David isn’t sure what to think after that.

He spends the evening either in bed trying to sleep or in the bathroom horking up nothing. He insists Patrick go spend time with his parents. He’s not going to be pleasant company, and not having Patrick around to watch him puke a million times will save himself some dignity.

David can’t blame the Brewers if they find his isolation rude. He could at least go downstairs and say hi, right? Well... he’s barely managing to scrounge up the motivation to head to the bathroom when he’s feeling sick. He’d just camp in there if it wouldn’t monopolize the upstairs bathroom.

The guilt of staying in their house and having yet to have a conversation with them just adds to his misery.

He moans, curls up, feeling particularly sorry for himself.

David jolts suddenly at a knock on the door. Which is confusing, because he can still hear Patrick’s voice downstairs.

“Hello...?” He eyes the door cautiously, wondering if he’s about to be murdered by a really discrete home invader.

The door opens, followed by a soft voice. “Hi, David,”

No intruder, just Marcy.

David sits up immediately and makes an attempt to fix his hair. He’d like to look at least slightly less infectious and terrible.

Marcy Brewer carries the same air of comfort as his husband. David finds himself feeling at ease as she steps carefully into the room.

“How are you? Patrick said you weren’t feeling well, I thought I’d check on you while the boys are talking.”

Well, he feels horrible, David wants to say. He feels drained physically and emotionally from doing nothing but throw up since he got here. David puts on a smile.

“I’m fine,” he says. It comes easily to him after spending a lifetime lying to people about his feelings.

“Are you sure?” she asks, expression so full of worry. David hears all of those stories of mothers being able to see right through you. Is that what’s happening here, he wonders?

Still, he nods. “Yeah, yes. I’m just sorry that I brought whatever this is to your home, Mrs. Brewer.”

She takes a few steps closer to the bed, looking almost as hesitant as he feels. “Well, first, we’re not worried about that,” she assures. “And you can call me Marcy, David. You’re my son-in-law,” she chuckles.

David nods, huffing out a laugh. “Right.”

She wrings her hands for a moment before speaking again. “If you’ve been throwing up, you might want something to eat or drink to keep you from getting dehydrated.”

He knows that, rationally. He hasn’t eaten since dinner last night and all he’s had was that horrible coffee. But putting anything else in his stomach means possibly throwing up again, and that wouldn’t be as much of a concern if he wasn’t in someone else’s home.

“I’m... I’m okay, but- thank you, um... Marcy.”

The name brings a smile to her face. “Okay,” she says, “Maybe I’ll send Patrick up with some stuff, just in case.”

He can’t help but return the smile, despite how awful he feels.

Patrick’s reading, the lamp from his side of the bed mercifully not reaching David enough to disturb him. All he has the energy for right now is to lie still, watch his husband turn the pages of his book, and try to will his stomach to stop making noise.

“Ugh.”
