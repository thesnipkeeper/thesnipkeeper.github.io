Winter Swim

Being immortal gave Henry a natural apathy toward life-threatening moments. He was the one more likely to interfere during a robbery, for example, and get shot while he was helping others out of the store.

Henry forgot to have a little more self-preservation during winter when waking up in the middle of the river was less-than-ideal. Thankfully it was nighttime, easy to hide and not as many people out and about.

He shivered violently as he waited for Abe, wondering if he’d die again, this time from frostbite, by the time he got there.

Finally, a car pulled up, and he raced inside, quickly starting to pull on the clothes Abe brought him.

“Jesus, Henry, are you insane? You need to be more careful this time of year, you know that. Have you ever died of pneumonia? Because you’re about to.”

Henry rolled his eyes. “Oh, p-please.” Alright, probably not the time to argue when his teeth were chattering. He pulled on the long-sleeved shirt, something loose and warm (thank you, Abe) and wrapped himself in the blanket that they kept in the car purely for this purpose.

He rubbed his nose as he was finally gaining feeling in it. Disgustingly, his hand came back wet.

“I’m serious, Henry. What if I can’t make it here in time and-”

“And I die?”

Abe sighed. “Yeah yeah, I see your point. Let’s just get you home.”

Abe shoved him at the couch, and Henry had no problem wrapping himself even warmer. He was still shivering, still freezing. He listened to his son make tea while he tried desperately to get warm.

Henry sniffled, igniting a sudden itch in his sinuses. He had no time to cover it with anything but his hand.

Suddenly, there was a tissue hanging in front of his face.

“Mm. See? What’d I tell you?”

Henry accepted it and wiped his nose thoroughly. “Cold weather doesn’t make you sick, Abe. I’m fine,” he lied. He could feel the cold virus swarming in his system. 

A cup of tea was pressed into his hands a moment later. “I know that. Just take it easy tonight.”

Henry had no problem with that idea.

His sniffling was becoming more frequent by the minute. He could feel Abe’s eyes on him as he pulled another tissue from the box in time to catch a sneeze.

“Bless you.”

“Abe...”

“What? I didn’t say a thing.”

Henry sighed. It was true, you couldn’t catch cold from being cold. But you could catch cold, and then make it worse by being in the cold.

“I may not have been feeling my best when I died,” Henry finally admitted.

Abe dropped the book in his hands, looking at Henry dumbfounded. 

It was something funny about Henry’s immortality that they’d both learned over the years.

Apparently nothing could cure the common cold. 

Not even death.

“God damn it, Henry.”

“Those people could have died if I didn’t help them. You know that.”

Abe sighed heavily, eyes dropping down to the abandoned book in his lap. “I know.”

Henry started shivering again before bed, and this time it wasn’t because he was cold. He let Abe lay a hand on his forehead, knowing that this wasn’t particularly good. 

He was a healthy man. It should have been fine. The cold might’ve gone easy on him had he not been swimming in a freezing river while he was ill.

Henry sneezed again. Abe frowned.

“I know I can’t convince you from staying home from work tomorrow,” He was right, he could never. “But at least go a little easy on yourself?”

“I’m a doctor. I know how to take care of my body.” Henry sniffled.

Abe scoffed. “Alright, fine. Goodnight, Henry.”

He smiled. “Goodnight. Abe...”

“Mm?”

“I love you.”

Abe gave him the same look he used to give him as a teenager when he said that. “Yeah yeah, I love you too, dad.”
