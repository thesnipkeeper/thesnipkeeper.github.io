Sounds From the Corner

Ted’s alone for his drinking tonight, which isn’t as bad as he’d expected it to be. The tavern isn’t too busy, but there’s a pleasant murmur of conversation and glass-clinking going on around him as he sits at the bar. It’s enough for him to drink happily without getting too much into his own head.

To be able to eavesdrop on multiple conversations is also proving to be a perfect way to wind down for the evening. There’s a couple at the table behind him having a tense conversation about whether they can afford to move, and two men at the other end of the bar complaining about their boss. And then there’s the sneezer.

Some poor soul in the back corner has contracted what sounds like the most miserable cold on planet earth. Ted can hear a sneeze every five minutes, sometimes followed by a rather wet blow into a bar napkin. They sniffle repeatedly until the next sneeze happens. It’s a pretty easy pattern to follow. As much as he wants to know what this person looks like, he doesn’t have the courage to look back, and they’d probably appreciate some privacy in their condition anyway.

The couple leaves without saying a word to each other, and the two men order another round of beers.

Ted winces at the sound of a wet sneeze. They blow their nose again and give an exhausted sniffle, as if it hasn’t relieved their snottiness one bit.

Mae brings him his second beer and looks behind him with a cringe. She shakes her head, either in disgust or sympathy, and goes back to work.

Okay, he just has to look now. He’s gotta.

He does.

And before Ted’s even aware of what he’s doing, he’s off of the bar stool and heading in that very direction, drink in hand.

“Trent Crimm, The Independent!” He announces.

Trent is leaned on the table, a hand supporting his head as he reads over something on a notepad. He looks like he’s been breathing through his mouth for a while, and his nose and eyes both have a pink-ish irritated hue to them. He doesn’t smile, in fact Ted is pretty sure the main looks more annoyed than he already did.

“Ah, Coach Lasso,” he sniffs lightly, a damp sound that says his nose is actively running and he hasn’t done anything about it quite yet. The table is littered with crumpled napkins that served that very purpose.

“Evenin’,” Ted nods. “Lookin’ a little rough there, friend.”

Trent sighs. “Oh, it’s... nothing, a bit of a cold.” He clears his throat and sits back against the chair. “Work still needs to be done.” It’s a wonder the man’s still upright. He looks exhausted.

“Right, yeah. That’s a good can-do attitude!”

He nods.

There’s a moment of awkward silence, and Ted shoves his right hand into his pocket and takes a sip of beer.

“I should get back to it,” Trent says dryly. He sniffles, almost too quiet to be heard.

“Of course! I’ll let you get back to work! Have a good night, and all that,” he says with a pleasant smile. He starts to walk away, and he does have every intent of leaving the ailing man alone to finish his work. (Trent must’ve been waiting for him to leave, because Ted hears him blowing his nose as soon as he turns his back) But he can’t help but take the few steps back over to Trent’s table. “It’s just-”

Trent sighs irritably, though it doesn’t sound nearly as threatening when it’s followed by a much-needed sniffle.

“Shouldn’t you be in bed? You might make yourself sicker, bein’ all out and about like this.”

There’s a hint of a smile on Trent’s lips. “I appreciate the concern, I do,” and this time, he sounds sincere. “But this,” he taps the paper with his pen, “Really needs to get done.”

“In that case, let me buy you a hot toddy.”

He blinks. “... what?”

“Y’all have those here, right? The hot drink with the lemon and-”

“No, I-” he huffs. “I know what a hot toddy is. I’m not going to let you buy me a drink.”

“Why not?”

Trent sniffs and runs the back of his hand under his nostrils in a quick swipe. “Ted, I can’t let you-”

“But you can!”

Trent gives a long sigh as he walks off toward the bar, which turns into a cough that has Ted wincing.
