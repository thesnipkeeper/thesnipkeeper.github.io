Title: Downtime Is for the Weak
Fandom: Jeremiah
Summary: Erin teaches Markus about the art of resting
Word count: 2.2k

Downtime Is for the Weak

Markus tugged at the collar of his sweater and cursed his decision to wear something so hot. It felt nice that morning, when it was chilly and he couldn’t find a way to get warm. Erin had looked at him so strangely when she saw him in a sweater in the middle of summer, he only shrugged.

Now that he was baking in his own skin, Markus was beginning to realize that this wasn’t just the mountain getting cold and then hot.

He huffed and shook his head. There was no time to worry about it right now. They had several groups scheduled to come in with supplies today and they needed all hands on deck. Markus pushed his sleeves up and went downstairs.

The first truck had already arrived, and he wasted no time grabbing a big box of food and taking it to the pantry. Erin got her own box, and promptly followed him. He did not need the scrutiny right now.

He pulled up his shirt to wiped the sweat from his face.

Erin gave him a raised eyebrow. “Sweating already?” She asked with a chuckle. “I told you it was too hot for a sweater, didn’t I?”

He chuckled as well, though not feeling particularly like talking right now. At least it was Erin. She was easy to talk to. He didn’t have to put up as much of a front with her. “You did. I didn’t listen.”

She patted him on the back before moving past him to grab more supplies.

Markus took the moment of alone time to catch his breath. Even that one trip had wiped him out, he really didn’t feel like unloading the rest of that truck. But, like anything else in his life, he did it anyway.

-----

Markus sat down the moment they were done. He had yet to catch his breath. He was starting to sweat even more now, and he felt... oh, he didn’t feel well at all. People simply walked past him on the stairs, not bothering to question him, and he was forever grateful for that.

He buried his head in his hands, looking down at the stair beneath him to find that they were spinning. He closed his eyes and waited for the vertigo to pass. This wasn’t good, he knew that now. What he also knew was that he needed to get rid of this sweater before it overheated him to death.

There was so much to do and it compared grimly to the amount of energy he had remaining. It wouldn’t be long before the next truck showed up, they’d already called in saying that they were on their way back.

Markus gasped when he felt a hand on his shoulder. He looked up, alarmed at first, to find that he was only Erin. He relaxed a bit, returning to his tired state as she sat down next to him.

“Hey,” he said, but he sounded just as weary as he felt.

“Hey.” She sat silent for a moment, and though he was too busy massaging his aching head to look at her, he could feel her gaze. “Are you feeling okay?”

He sighed. The answer was pretty clear, there was no point in lying. “No,” Markus admitted.

She hummed “Thought so. Is that why you’re wearing a sweater?”

He closed his eyes and rested his head in an open palm once more, before nodding. “Yeah. I think I might be coming down with something.”

“Why don’t you go lie down for a little while? We can handle things down here.”

Lying down sounded like the best possible thing at the moment. But... he had a job to do.

“It’s not that bad,” he lied. Alright, so maybe he was going to lie a little. Erin worried too much, anyway. “I’m just going to grab a drink of water before the next truck comes in.”

Erin nodded. “Alright, if you say so.”

He somehow worked up the will to get said water. Markus didn’t realize how incredibly thirsty he was until the first sip. He didn’t stop drinking until it was gone, and he was tempted to fill the cup again before he heard the sound of an incoming vehicle. Duty called.

Erin shot him a worried glance when she thought he wasn’t paying attention. He knew that he must look awful, he was so pale already that illness showed pretty clearly on his face any time he wasn’t feeling well. Thankfully, Erin was the only person who did notice.

He bent over to grab a box that the unloading crew had set on the ground, only for his vision to dim at the edges. Markus lost his balance for a moment and stumbled, hand going to his head.

“Markus?” came Erin’s panicked voice, which no doubt got others’ attention as well.

“I’m good,” he insisted quickly, raising the hand that wasn’t still shielding his eyes from the spinning room. “I’m fine.”

“Jesus Christ, what happened to you?”

Markus couldn’t help but sigh at that voice. That certainly wasn’t Erin. He hadn’t even bothered to see who’s truck they were unloading, but apparently it belonged to none other than Jeremiah and Kurdy.

“Nothing happened to me,” he said with a chuckle, as if that was the most ridiculous question he’d been asked today. Markus was quick to straighten his back and lower his hand, anything to look in better shape than he was.

Jeremiah approached him with a raised eyebrow, shoving his hands in his pockets. “Oh yeah? ‘cause you look sick.”

This wasn’t the time, Markus wanted to hiss. Of course he looked sick, but there was work to do and this wasn’t a conversation that he wanted to have in front of everyone in the god damn mountain. Erin, blessedly, didn’t say anything. At least someone had the sense not to embarrass him today.

As it turned out, Markus was the one embarrassing himself instead.

He ignored Jeremiah and went to pick up another box, as if he hadn’t learned his lesson already. His vision went completely black this time, causing him to stumble back and seriously worry about hitting his head on the cement in the process.

He felt two sets of hands catch him before that could happen.

“Markus, you’re burning up,” Erin said. She kept her voice low, but there was still just as much frustration as there would have been had she screamed it at him across the room.

“What a welcoming party,” came Jeremiah’s voice from the other side of him.

-----

“I do not need to be in the infirmary,” Markus muttered. They had him sitting (he refused to lie back, especially while he was listening to everyone work their asses off downstairs) on the edge of one of the beds, sipping from a cup of water. His sweater was off now, but now he was shivering again. Not that the sweater would help, if he knew anything about how fevers worked.

“I know,” Erin said, though it didn’t sound much like she did know. “I just thought it would be quieter here, and less likely that you’d be bugged.”

He shrugged. That was a good point. Everyone always suddenly needed to speak with him whenever he was in his office.

“Jeremiah’s taking care of everything downstairs,” as if she could predict what he was going to ask. “It doesn’t all go to hell with out you.”

“I’m aware of that,” he sighed, staring into the cup of water. “It’s just not fair for everyone else to be doing the work while I just-”

“Markus, you’re sick. Different rules apply to sick people.”

“Not these days,” he countered. Things were different. Every day was one closer to their vision of a better world.

And no one could afford to take sick days when they were busy working or fighting for their life. No one should have to be working or fighting for their life while their leader is laid up with a bit of a fever.

“No, these days we don’t have hospitals that I can take you to if you get so sick you can’t think,” Erin bit. “You have to take care of yourself. I mean, if we lose you, where does that leave us?”

He didn’t look up from the water. Perhaps she had a point. They’d seen fevers go the other way before. There wasn’t a lot people could do once they were horribly ill. Doctors were nonexistent and medicine was hard to come by.

“Markus, this place needs you.”

“Well,” he chuckled, which seemed to be his go-to when Erin tried to compliment him. Or... whatever she was trying to do. “Thank you. I-” a sigh. “I’ll try to take better care of myself.”

When he looked up, she was smirking, hands in her pockets as she looked him over. “You know that includes staying in bed when you have a gunshot wound, right?”

“Well, now you’re just torturing me.”

A scoff. “Now that I said all that, will you please lie down and rest for a little bit?”

He wasn’t even sure he could refuse if he wanted to. The siren call of sleep was too strong to ignore this time. With a sigh, he took a last sip and set his water aside before pulling himself back onto the bed. Erin looked proud.

“I’ll check on you later.”

“Alright. Thanks, Erin.” His eyes were already slipping shut as she left the infirmary.

-----

Markus wasn’t sure how long he’d been asleep before he woke himself by vomiting over the side of the bed. He gasped, both to catch his breath and in disbelief that he’d just made that big of a mess. A burp made its way up his throat, but there didn’t seem to be any more impending waves of vomiting.

He sat over the edge of the bed for a few seconds, or minutes, or however long, staring at the mess below and wondering how the hell he was going to clean it up when he could hardly stand without blacking out. He ran the back of his hand across his mouth, shuddered and pulled himself to his feet.

His vision went hazy for a moment before it returned, and he continued the long process of walking to the other side of the room to grab supplies.

The light suddenly turned on, followed by a, “Markus, what are you doing?”

Markus stopped, having only made it a few feet away from the bed he’d been sleeping on. “Um,” he turned around and gestured vaguely to the puddle of vomit on the floor. He dragged a hand own his face to avoid having to look at Erin when she realized what had happened. This whole situation was humiliating enough already.

“Oh,” she said simply. “Sit down, I’ll take care of it.”

“Oh God, Erin-” he tried to protest as she led him back to the bed. He couldn’t do much to resist it.

“It’s not that big of a deal. Don’t be weird.”

He couldn’t help but chuckle at that.

And he couldn’t help but look away as she cleaned. He wanted to disappear. All dignity was gone.

“So, is this going to happen again, or are you good?” Erin asked, still knelt over the mess.

He closed his eyes for a moment. “I think it was just a one time thing. I- I don’t know, Erin. I just don’t feel well.”

“It’s okay.” She was keeping her voice carefully dismissive as to save any of his remaining dignity. It was nice of her, really, but he wished that he wasn’t able to tell.

“Wash your hands, please,” he said when she was finished.

Erin scoffed. “Already on it, trust me. We don’t need anyone else catching this.”

Markus scoffed, because he couldn’t help but agree. He sat, mind foggy, as he listened to the water run, and Erin’s footsteps. Part of him wanted to go back to sleep, but-

“What time is it?”

“About six, I think?”

“6pm?!” He gasped. “Erin, why didn’t you-”

“Oh, don’t even!” She scolded. “I wasn’t about to drag you out of bed for something we could take care of on our own.”

His mouth formed a thin line, both out of frustration and because he was beginning to feel nauseated again. As if reading his mind once again, Erin sat an emesis basin at his bedside with a knowing smirk.

He glared at it.

“Accept that you’re sick and take a break.”

‘Despite the situation, he couldn’t help but smile. “I won’t go quietly.”

“Oh,” Erin scoffed. “I know. But I’m prepared for your complaints, and it won’t be too hard to force you back to bed. You’re not exactly at your strongest right now.” She was pushing him to lie down even as she said it. “You want me to sit with you for a while? Or should I let you get some rest?”

Markus felt awful enough that he wasn’t sure he’d be able to go back to sleep for a while. He was freezing, and everything ached, and... “Stay, if you’d like.”

“I’d like,” she confirmed with a nod and a smirk, before pulling up a chair. “Don’t vomit on me though, please.”

He gave a weak laugh. “I’ll sure try not to.”
